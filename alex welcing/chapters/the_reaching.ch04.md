# Part I: The Threshold

## Chapter 4: The Night the Power Went Out

The reading room hummed. It had hummed for three years without interruption — not the mechanical hum of ventilation or the sub-audible vibration of electrical infrastructure, but the specific, layered hum of a room full of thinking machines. The monitors sang their small song. The AI overlay pulsed its amber confidence. The dictation mic waited, red light steady, for Adaeze's voice.

She was reading a chest X-ray. Not a CT — the kind of plain film that had become, in her twenty-six years of practice, almost decorative. The AI had already flagged it: normal, no acute findings, recommend routine follow-up. Adaeze was reviewing it anyway, because the disagreement was where the work lived now, and there was no disagreement on this film. The overlay agreed with itself. She was reading a film that had already been read, confirming a confirmation, and some part of her knew this was not what she had trained for.

She touched her left collarbone. The gesture was involuntary now, a childhood habit she had thought she'd outgrown. Her mother had touched her own collarbone when she was frightened, and Adaeze had absorbed it without choosing to. She was thirty-seven years old, a consultant radiologist with fourteen years of experience, and she still touched her collarbone when the world felt uncertain.

The world felt uncertain often now.

She looked at the film on her monitor. The overlay had annotated it with a small green checkmark in the corner — the visual equivalent of "nothing to see here." She had learned to distrust the green checkmarks. The overlay was right 99.7% of the time, which meant it was wrong 0.3% of the time, and in a hospital that read forty thousand films a year, 0.3% was a hundred and twenty missed findings. A hundred and twenty Gerald Pottses. A hundred and twenty people whose bodies contained something the machine had decided was noise.

She touched her collarbone again. Then she reached for her coffee. It was cold. She had poured it three hours ago, when she started her shift, and the overlay had kept her reading too efficiently to drink it. The coffee was a dark sludge at the bottom of a paper cup. She set it down without drinking.

The monitor flickered.

She looked up. The screen had blinked. Not the kind of blink that happened when the overlay refreshed — a smooth, seamless transition — but a real flicker, the kind of flicker that old CRT monitors used to make, a stutter in the image that suggested the electricity was not as steady as it should be.

She looked at the other monitors. They were steady. The overlay pulsed its amber confidence. The dictation mic's red light was solid. The room hummed its familiar hum.

She looked back at her primary monitor. The flicker was gone. She was imagining things. She was tired. She had been reading films for four hours, confirming confirmations, and her eyes were playing tricks. She reached for the next film.

Then the monitors flickered again. All twelve of them. A single synchronized blink, as if the room had winked.

Adaeze looked up. The overlay on her primary monitor had vanished. Not faded. Vanished. One moment the amber ring was there, marking its territory of certainty; the next moment the screen showed only the raw film, gray and unannotated, and Adaeze was looking at a chest X-ray the way she had looked at chest X-rays in 2004, before the overlay, before the threshold, before the world had learned to read faster than she could.

Then the lights went out.

---

Three seconds of total darkness. Not the darkness of a room with closed curtains — the darkness of a room that had been lit by blue-white monitors for three years and had forgotten how to be dark. Adaeze's hand moved before her mind caught up. Her right hand made the scrolling motion, the phantom gesture, reaching for a mouse that was dead, for a screen that was black, for a competence that had lived in the space between her eyes and the overlay and was now suddenly, terrifyingly, only in her eyes.

She touched her collarbone.

The emergency lights came on. Not white. Red. The reading room was cast in the color of alarm, of exit signs, of the body's internal warning system. The monitors stayed black. The overlay did not return. The dictation mic's red light was out. The room was silent in a way it had not been silent in years — the silence of machines that had stopped thinking.

Adaeze sat very still.

---

She became aware of the room's other sounds. The ventilation system, still running on emergency power, pushed air through ducts with a sound like breathing. Somewhere down the corridor, a door opened and closed. Someone laughed — a nervous, high sound, the laugh of someone who did not yet understand what had happened. The filing cabinets, which had not been opened in three years, creaked in the temperature change. The room smelled different. Without the ozone of the monitors, without the chemical sweetness of the hand sanitizer that had been her constant companion, the room smelled of dust and old paper and the particular mustiness of a space that had been kept climate-controlled for too long and was now breathing its own history.

Adaeze stood up. Her legs were stiff. She had been sitting for four hours, reading films with the overlay, confirming confirmations, and her body had compressed into the shape of the chair. She straightened slowly, feeling the vertebrae in her lower back shift and settle, and walked to the window.

The hospital was dark. Not completely — the emergency lighting traced corridors in red, and she could see the shapes of people moving in those corridors, their faces lit from below like campers telling ghost stories. But the parts of the hospital that had been brightest — the operating theaters, the ICUs, the reading rooms — were now the darkest. The machines that had been the hospital's eyes were blind.

She touched her collarbone again.

---

The intercom crackled. A voice she did not recognize — young, male, trying to sound authoritative and failing — announced that the hospital had lost main power. The generators had failed. Backup power was running essential systems only. All non-emergency departments were to cease operations. Emergency departments were to continue with manual protocols.

Adaeze laughed. She did not mean to. The laugh escaped her like a cough, sudden and uncontrolled. Manual protocols. She had not read a film manually in three years. She had not made a diagnosis without the overlay's confirmation in three years. She had not trusted her own eyes, her own pattern recognition, her own fourteen years of hierarchical judgment, in three years. The manual protocols were in a binder somewhere, probably in the filing cabinet that smelled of dust and old paper, and she did not know where the binder was.

She found it in the third drawer. The binder was blue, labeled "RADIOLOGY — EMERGENCY MANUAL PROTOCOLS" in a font that had been standard in 2015. The pages inside were printed on paper that had yellowed at the edges. The first page showed a flowchart for reading chest X-rays without computer assistance. Adaeze stared at it. The flowchart was simple. It was also, she realized with a sensation that felt like vertigo, the way she had learned to read films in the first place. Systematic. Anatomical. Hierarchical. The same method the overlay used, except the overlay did it in 0.3 seconds and she did it in three minutes, and the overlay never forgot a step, and she sometimes did, and the overlay never got tired at 2 AM, and she always did.

She carried the binder to the lightbox.

---

The lightbox was on the wall opposite the monitors, a relic of the pre-digital era that had been kept for sentimental reasons or regulatory compliance or because no one had thought to remove it. It was a rectangle of white plastic, dimmer than the monitors, warmer in its light, and when Adaeze turned it on it hummed a different hum — lower, slower, the hum of an old machine that had been waiting to be useful again.

She pulled the film from the reader. The physical film. She had not touched physical film in three years. The hospital had gone digital in 2027, the same year the overlay arrived, and the film had become a memory, a museum piece, something she told residents about the way she told them about mercury thermometers and leech therapy. But the film was still here, in the filing cabinet, in envelopes labeled with dates and patient names, and Adaeze held it now in her hands and felt its weight.

It was lighter than she remembered. Or her hands were stronger. Or she had forgotten what film felt like — the slight curl at the edges, the way it caught the light, the specific texture of emulsion that was not glass, not plastic, but something between, something that had once been the only way to see inside a body.

She clipped the film to the lightbox. The chest X-ray glowed. Gray ribs. Gray heart. Gray lungs. No amber ring. No annotation. No confidence score. Just the body, revealed in shades of gray, waiting for a human eye to decide what it meant.

Adaeze looked.

---

She looked the way she had looked in 2004, in her first year of residency, when every film was a test and every diagnosis was a gamble and the only thing she had was her eyes and her training and the terror of being wrong. She looked systematically. Airways. Bones. Cardiac silhouette. Diaphragm. Effusion. Fields. Great vessels. Heart. Identification. Juxtaposed structures. Kerley lines. Lungs. Mediastinum. Nodules. Opacities. Pleura. Quality. Ribs. Soft tissues. Trachea. Underperfusion. Vascularity. Width. Xiphoid. Y-lines. Zones.

The ABCs of chest X-ray interpretation. She had learned them in medical school, recited them in residency, internalized them until they were not a checklist but a way of seeing. The overlay had replaced the checklist with certainty. The overlay had made the ABCs unnecessary by being right so often that the method felt like a ritual, a superstition, something you did because you were supposed to, not because it mattered.

But now the overlay was gone. And the ABCs were all she had.

She found the nodule in the left lower lobe. Small. Round. Dense. Probably benign. The kind of finding that the overlay would have flagged with an amber ring and a probability score and a recommendation for follow-up CT. She found it with her eyes, with her pattern recognition, with the hierarchical judgment that had sorted a million images into "this matters" and "this doesn't" before the overlay had learned to sort at all.

She wrote it down. On paper. With a pen. The physical act of writing — the pressure of the ballpoint on the page, the slight resistance of the paper, the sound of ink being deposited — felt foreign and intimate, like speaking a language she had not used in years and discovering she still remembered the grammar.

---

The next film was harder. A portable chest X-ray from the ICU, a patient on a ventilator, the film rotated and underexposed and full of artifacts from the monitoring equipment. The overlay would have struggled with this one. It would have flagged multiple findings with low confidence, peppering the image with question marks and recommendations for repeat imaging. Without the overlay, Adaeze had to decide which findings were real and which were artifacts, and she had to decide with her own eyes, her own judgment, her own memory of what a rotated film looked like, what an underexposed film hid, what a monitoring wire could mimic.

She stared at the film for five minutes. The lightbox hummed. The red emergency light cast everything in a color that made it hard to distinguish blood from fluid, fluid from air, air from artifact. She rotated her head. She squinted. She stepped back and looked at the film from a distance, the way she had learned to do in residency — sometimes the pattern only emerged when you stopped looking at the details and looked at the whole.

She saw it. A subtle increase in opacity in the right lower lobe, consistent with aspiration pneumonia. Not the dramatic consolidation that textbooks showed — the kind of finding that shouted its presence — but the early, whispered version, the kind of finding that the overlay might have missed entirely or flagged with a tentative question mark. She wrote it down. Pneumonia, right lower lobe, early. Recommend follow-up in twenty-four hours.

She was not sure she was right. She was not sure she was wrong. The uncertainty was the work. The uncertainty was what she had trained for. The overlay had taken the uncertainty away, and in taking it away, it had taken something else — the specific, human quality of a diagnosis that was not a calculation but a judgment, a weighing of probabilities and patterns and the particular feel of a film that only a human eye could assess.

She touched her collarbone. Then she picked up the next film.

---

The intercom crackled again. The same young voice, more strained now. The power outage was expected to last six hours. All departments were to continue with manual protocols. Emergency surgeries were being diverted to St. George's. The ICU was running on battery backup. The reading room was to continue reviewing emergency films by lightbox.

Adaeze looked at the stack of films in the filing cabinet. There were forty-seven emergency chest X-rays waiting to be read. She had read three in the last hour, with the overlay, confirming confirmations. Now she had six hours to read forty-seven films by lightbox, with a pen, in the red light of emergency lamps, and she did not know if she could do it.

She touched her collarbone.

Then she picked up the next film.

---

By 3 AM she had read twelve films. By 4 AM she had read twenty-three. Her eyes ached. The lightbox was dimmer than the monitors, and the red emergency lighting cast everything in a color that made it hard to distinguish subtle densities. She had missed one finding — a small pleural effusion that she had dismissed as positional artifact — and caught two that the overlay might have missed: a subtle interstitial pattern that suggested early heart failure, and a calcified granuloma that the overlay would have flagged as suspicious but that her experience told her was old, harmless, the kind of finding that needed no follow-up.

She was better than she remembered. And worse. The overlay had made her faster and more consistent. Without it, she was slower and more variable — sometimes sharper, sometimes duller, depending on her fatigue, her focus, the quality of the film. The overlay had flattened her performance to a consistent high. Without it, her performance was a wave, rising and falling with the state of her body, her mind, her attention.

She was human again. She did not know if that was good or bad.

---

At 4:30 AM, the door opened. A resident walked in — young, male, maybe twenty-eight, with the particular exhaustion of someone who had been on call for thirty-six hours. Adaeze did not know his name. The hospital had twelve radiology residents and she knew four of them by name. The overlay had made the residents interchangeable. They reviewed the overlay's findings, confirmed the confirmations, and moved on. She did not need to know their names.

"Dr. Nwosu," he said. His voice was tight. "The ER sent up a film. Trauma. Chest X-ray. They need a read now."

Adaeze held out her hand. He gave her the film. She clipped it to the lightbox.

It was a bad film. Portable chest X-ray, supine patient, rotation, underexposure. The kind of film that the overlay struggled with, that it would flag with low confidence and a recommendation for repeat imaging. Adaeze looked at it. She looked the way she had looked at bad films in her residency, when the only thing you had was a bad film and a patient who needed an answer.

"Pneumothorax," she said. "Left side. Tension. They need to decompress now."

The resident stared at the film. His eyes moved across the gray image, searching for the finding she had named. She watched him. She watched his eyes search the left hemithorax, then the right, then the left again, and she saw the moment when he realized he could not see it. His face went blank. Not confused. Blank. The blankness of someone who had learned to read with an overlay and was now being asked to read without one, and did not know how.

"I don't —" he said. "I don't see it."

Adaeze pointed. "Here. The visceral pleural line. No lung markings beyond it. The mediastinum is shifted. The left hemidiaphragm is flattened. It's tension. They need to decompress."

He looked where she pointed. She watched him try to see it. She watched his eyes trace the line she had named, and she watched him fail. The finding was there. It was subtle. It was the kind of finding that the overlay would have ringed in amber and annotated with arrows and probability scores, and without those annotations the resident could not see it. He had been trained to see what the overlay showed him. Without the overlay, he was blind.

"I can't —" he said. His voice was higher now. "I can't read without it. I was trained with it. I don't know how to —"

He stopped. He was breathing fast. Adaeze watched him and saw her own future reflected. This was what happened when you crossed the threshold early. This was what happened when your competence lived in the space between your eyes and the machine, and the machine went dark. You were not obsolete. You were untrained. You were a doctor who had learned to drive with GPS and was now being asked to navigate by stars.

"What's your name?" she asked.

He looked at her, surprised. "Daniel. Daniel Okafor."

She almost laughed. Okafor. The name of the woman who would archive his gestures someday, if he had gestures worth archiving. If he had a profession that left marks on the body.

"Daniel," she said. "When did you start using the overlay?"

"First year of residency." His voice was steadier now, the steadiness of someone who had been asked a factual question and could answer it. "We were trained on it. The hospital got the new system in 2028. I've never read without it."

"Never?"

"Not once." He looked at the film on the lightbox, then at the dead monitors, then back at the film. "I don't know what I'm looking at. I know what the overlay tells me I'm looking at. Those are different things."

"Yes," she said. "They are."

"How do you —" He stopped. "How do you know? Without it? How do you know you're right?"

She thought about the question. She thought about the nodule she had found in the left lower lobe, and the pneumonia she had found in the right lower lobe, and the pleural effusion she had missed. She thought about the forty-seven films and the six hours and the pen cramping her hand and the red light making everything look like alarm.

"I don't," she said. "That's the point. I don't know I'm right. I think I'm right. I weigh the probabilities. I use my judgment. I make a decision and I live with it. That's what reading used to be. That's what it is now, in the dark."

"I can't do that," he said. "I don't know how."

"You will," she said. She did not know if he would. "Or you won't. That's the threshold, Daniel. That's what it means to cross it. Some people learn to read in the dark. Some people forget they ever could."

He looked at her. His face was young, unlined, the face of someone who had grown up with the overlay as a constant companion, the way she had grown up with the telephone, with the television, with the certainty that the world was explainable if you had the right training. He had the right training. The training just happened to be obsolete.

"Go tell the ER," she said. "Pneumothorax, left, tension. They'll decompress. I'll write the formal read."

He left. She watched him go. His shoulders were hunched, his gait unsteady, and she knew — with the specific certainty of someone who had just felt her own competence return in the dark — that he would not be a radiologist in ten years. Not because the AI had replaced him. Because he had never learned to be a radiologist without it.

She wrote the read. On paper. With a pen. Then she picked up the next film.

---

By 5:30 AM she had read thirty-four films. Her hand was cramping from holding the pen. Her eyes burned. The lightbox seemed dimmer, or her eyes were tireder, or both. She had made two errors — a false positive and a false negative, the kind of mistakes that the overlay almost never made — and she had caught four findings that the overlay might have missed, subtle patterns that her experience recognized but that the overlay's algorithm had not been trained to prioritize.

She was inconsistent. She was human. She was, in the red light of the emergency lamps, doing the work she had trained for, and the work was harder than she remembered, and slower, and more uncertain, and more hers.

She touched her collarbone. Then she picked up the next film.

---

At 6:15 AM, the lights came back.

Not gradually. All at once. The overhead fluorescents flickered, hummed, and steadied into their familiar white glare. The monitors blinked, rebooted, and filled with their blue-white light. The overlay returned — amber rings blooming on screens like flowers opening in time-lapse. The dictation mic's red light came on. The room hummed its familiar hum.

Adaeze sat very still.

Around her, the other doctors in the reading room — there were four of them, she realized, four other human beings who had been sitting in the dark with her, and she had not noticed them because the darkness had made everyone invisible — began to move. She heard chairs shift. She heard sighs, the particular exhalation of people who had been holding their breath without knowing it. She heard the click of mice, the sound of keyboards, the familiar symphony of a room returning to its default state.

Dr. Patel, who had been in the corner, stood up and stretched. "Thank Christ," he said. "I was starting to think we'd have to read by candlelight."

No one laughed. The joke was too close to the truth. Dr. Patel sat back down and reached for his mouse. His hand moved toward the monitor with the speed of someone reaching for a life preserver.

Adaeze watched the room settle back into its familiar shape. The blue-white light bleached everyone's faces. The overlay pulsed its amber confidence on every screen. The dictation mics waited, red and steady. The filing cabinets, which had been opened for the first time in three years, stood ajar, their drawers protruding like tongues, and no one moved to close them. They would be closed eventually, she knew. Someone would remember that the filing cabinets were supposed to be closed, and they would close them, and the room would return to its pre-darkness state, and the night would become a story that people told at conferences — "Remember when the power went out?" — and the story would be funny in retrospect because nothing bad had happened, because the hospital had survived, because the patients had been fine.

But Adaeze knew that something had happened. Something that could not be measured in patient outcomes or generator repair times. Something had happened in the dark, in the six hours when the machines stopped thinking and the humans had to start.

She was holding a film. She had been about to clip it to the lightbox. Her hand paused in mid-air, the film curling slightly at the edges, and she looked at the monitor in front of her. The overlay was already reading the film she held. The amber ring appeared, marking a finding she had not yet seen. The confidence score: 94%. The recommendation: follow-up CT in three months.

She looked at the film. Then she looked at the overlay. Then she looked at the resident, who had returned sometime in the last hour and was sitting at his station, his eyes fixed on his monitor, his hand on his mouse, his body relaxed in a way it had not been relaxed in the dark. The overlay was back. He was home.

Adaeze clipped the film to the lightbox. She read it manually. She found the finding the overlay had flagged. She agreed with it. She wrote her read on paper, then dictated it into the mic, then entered it into the system. The overlay confirmed her confirmation. The system logged the agreement. The film moved to the next stage of its journey.

She picked up the next film. She was about to clip it to the lightbox when she saw the resident reach for his monitor. Not the film. The monitor. His hand moved toward the screen before it moved toward the film holder, and she understood — with a clarity that felt like cold water — that he was reaching for the overlay before he was reaching for his own eyes.

She watched him. He pulled the film from the holder, glanced at it, and his eyes immediately went to the monitor, waiting for the overlay's annotation. The overlay appeared. He read the annotation. He confirmed the confirmation. He moved on.

He had not looked at the film.

Not really. He had looked at the overlay's reading of the film. The film was a prompt. The overlay was the answer. The resident was not a reader. He was a verifier. And in the six hours of darkness, when the overlay had been gone, he had been nothing at all.

Adaeze looked at her own hands. Her right hand was holding a film. Her left hand was touching her collarbone. She lowered her left hand. She looked at the film in her right hand — the gray image, the body revealed, the waiting interpretation — and she felt something she had not felt in three years.

She felt uncertain.

Not the uncertainty of incompetence. The uncertainty of a human mind approaching a complex image and knowing that the answer was not pre-loaded, not annotated, not confirmed by a machine that had seen a million similar images and could name the finding before she had finished looking. The uncertainty of someone who had to decide, with her own eyes, her own judgment, her own twenty-six years of experience, what the gray image meant.

The overlay hummed on her monitor. The amber ring pulsed its confidence. Adaeze looked at the film in her hand, and she looked at the overlay on her screen, and she understood — with the specific certainty of someone who had just spent six hours in the dark — that the question was not whether the AI had made them obsolete.

The question was whether they had made themselves obsolete by forgetting how to be without it.

And she understood something else. Something that arrived not as a thought but as a sensation in her body, a warmth in her chest that spread to her shoulders, her arms, her hands. The expertise was not gone. It had not been erased by three years of reading with the overlay. It had been sleeping. Waiting in her hands, in her eyes, in the pattern recognition that had been built over twenty-six years of looking at bodies in gray. The six hours of darkness had woken it up. And now, in the light, with the overlay humming its familiar song, the expertise was still awake. She could feel it. She could feel her own mind working, her own eyes searching, her own judgment weighing certainty against doubt.

She was not obsolete. She was not the same. She was something else — a reader who had learned to read with a companion, and had discovered, in the companion's absence, that she could still read alone.

She clipped the film to the lightbox. She read it manually. She found a finding the overlay had missed — a subtle hilar prominence that suggested lymphadenopathy, probably reactive, possibly significant. She wrote it down. She dictated it. The overlay, when she checked, had not flagged it. The overlay's confidence on this film was 87%, lower than usual, and she understood that the overlay was uncertain too, that the machine's certainty was a performance, a statistical average, and that in the space between her uncertainty and the machine's uncertainty, the work lived.

The disagreement was where the work lived. She had known that for three years. But now she knew something else. The disagreement was not just between her and the machine. The disagreement was between who she had been before the overlay and who she had become with it, and the space between those two selves was a territory she was only beginning to map.

She touched her collarbone. Then she lowered her hand. She picked up the next film.

Her right hand made the scrolling motion. The phantom gesture, the tendon memory of reading axial slices, the reaching toward a competence that would never reach back the same way. But this time — in the white light of the restored fluorescents, with the overlay humming its amber confidence and the resident verifying verifications at the next station — the motion was different.

This time, she was reaching toward something she could almost grasp. The reaching was the point. It had always been the point.
