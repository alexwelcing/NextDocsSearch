# Part II: The Wilderness

## Chapter 16: The Correspondent

The journalist arrived on a Tuesday in January, the day after a storm had stripped the last leaves from the plane trees on Brixton Road. Adaeze watched her from the balcony — a woman in a wool coat the color of wet slate, consulting her phone, then looking up at the building with the specific expression of someone who had been here before and was not certain the place would match her memory.

Maya Thornton. Fifty-four. The same Maya who had sat in a café on Atlantic Road in 2027, her recorder on the table between them, her questions sharp and young and unaware that they were about to become the vocabulary of an era. Adaeze had been thirty-four then. She was sixty-three now. The difference was not merely years. The difference was weather — the same climate, but the storms had changed the landscape.

Adaeze went inside and put the kettle on. She did not hurry. Hurrying was a habit she had shed along with the white coat, along with the night shifts, along with the scrolling hand that had once been her signature and was now, she had accepted, simply her hand.

---

Maya's recorder was smaller now, a disc the size of a coin that she placed on the kitchen table without asking permission. The old Maya had asked. The new Maya assumed — not from rudeness, Adaeze noted, but from the erosion of etiquette that came from decades of interviewing people who no longer expected privacy.

"You don't mind," Maya said. It was not a question.

"I mind less than I did in 2027," Adaeze said. "That is not the same as not minding."

Maya smiled — a quick, professional smile that did not reach her eyes. Her eyes were tired. Adaeze recognized the tiredness. It was the tiredness of someone who had been asking the same question for twenty years and was beginning to suspect that the question was not about the people she interviewed. The question was about herself.

"I covered the Cartography," Maya said. "Delft. The Institute. I met Solène Diarra."

Adaeze poured tea. Her right hand, holding the pot, made the small downward scrolling motion it always made when Solène's name was spoken. She had not seen Solène in four years. The cartographer sent letters — actual letters, on paper, from places Adaeze could not visit — and Adaeze read them with her reading glasses pushed up into her silver hair, her cardigan buttoned to the throat, her body very still.

"Solène told me about you," Maya said. "She said you were the territory. She said the threshold generation had been living in the boundary all along, and the cartographers were only now learning to read the maps you had already written in your bodies."

"Solène is generous with her metaphors."

"Solène is precise. That's what frightens me."

Adaeze set down the pot. She looked at Maya — really looked, the way she had once looked at scans, the diagnostic gaze that assessed density and structure and the subtle gradients where normal became abnormal.

"You asked me a question in 2027," Adaeze said. "In the café on Atlantic Road. You asked what happens when the thing you do best is no longer needed. I answered with my hands. I described a weight."

"I remember."

"You asked Solène the same question."

Maya's hand, resting on the table, twitched. Not much. A millimeter. But Adaeze saw it.

"She answered the same way," Maya said. "The same weight. The same words, almost. 'The work is what I am. When the work stops, I will be a person who used to be a cartographer. And the used to be will be the heaviest part of me.'"

"She learned that from me."

"Or you both learned it from the same place."

Adaeze sipped her tea. The kitchen was warm. The trains passed below the window, their rhythm unchanged after thirty-six years. She had listened to those trains as a junior resident, as a consultant, as a woman who no longer had a hospital to go to. The trains did not care about thresholds. The trains only cared about rails.

"The question is not what I answered then or what Solène answers now," Adaeze said. "The question is whether the answer has changed, or whether the person answering has changed so gradually that she did not notice."

"Has it changed?"

Adaeze looked at her hands. They were steady. They had always been steady. The right hand, resting on the tablecloth, made the scrolling motion — thumb and index finger, downward, the phantom gesture of a radiologist reading axial slices. She did not try to stop it. She had stopped trying to stop it fifteen years ago.

"In 2027," Adaeze said, "I told you the weight was the point. I told you the reaching was all that remained. I was wrong. Not entirely wrong. But wrong enough that my wrongness matters."

Maya leaned forward. The recorder caught the shift — a soft scrape of wool against the chair back.

"The weight is not the point," Adaeze said. "The weight is the tide. It comes in. It goes out. When the tide is high, you think you will drown. When the tide is low, you think the water has left you forever. Neither is true. The tide is the truth. The learning to swim is the truth. The drowning and the not-drowning are both temporary."

She paused. Her hand scrolled. The motion was involuntary, beautiful, the ghost of a practice haunting the body that housed it.

"I have lived through the entire arc, Ms. Thornton. I have been the radiologist who was sure. I have been the radiologist who was surpassed. I have been the woman who grieved a career that did not fail but became unnecessary. I have been the witness to the cartographers who map the space where my grief used to live. And I am still here. The weight has not killed me. The reaching has not arrived anywhere. The tide has turned, and turned again, and I am still here."

Maya wrote something in her notebook — actual paper, actual pen, the tools of a journalist who had begun her career when screens were smaller and attention was different.

"What are you now?" Maya asked.

Adaeze considered. The question was the same. The answer was not.

"I am a woman who sits very still," she said. "I am a woman whose hand scrolls through scans that no longer exist. I am a woman who has learned that the boundary is not a moment you cross. It is a condition you inhabit. I am not a radiologist. I am not a cartographer. I am not anything that fits in your notebook, Ms. Thornton. I am the tide."

---

Maya put her notebook away. She looked out the window at the bare plane trees, at the trains, at the city that had changed around Adaeze without asking her permission.

"I want to show you something," Maya said. "In Rotterdam. A garden."

"I have seen gardens."

"Not this one. This one was built for the cognitive boundary. A landscape architect named Noor van der Berg designed it so that the body would feel what the cartographers feel. She built the threshold in grass and water and stone."

Adaeze's hand stopped scrolling. It rested on the tablecloth, the fingers slightly curled, the posture of a hand that had reached for something and was considering whether to reach again.

"When?" Adaeze asked.

"Tomorrow. I have a car."

Adaeze looked at her tea. It had gone cold. She had not noticed. The diagnostic gaze, still intact after all these years, still finding the detail that mattered — the temperature change, the color shift, the small sign that something had shifted while she was not paying attention.

"Yes," she said.

---

**Rotterdam**

They drove through the Channel Tunnel, emerging into a grey European afternoon that smelled of diesel and rain. Maya talked — about the Cartography Institute, about Solène's expeditions, about the blank map that hung in the Delft lobby and the cartographers who stood before it and wept without knowing why. Adaeze listened. She did not interrupt. Interruption was a habit of the young, and she was no longer young.

The Garden Between occupied 2.4 hectares of reclaimed industrial land along the Nieuwe Maas, south of the Erasmus Bridge. Adaeze saw it before they parked — a low wall of weathered brick, no signage, no map at the entrance. Three narrow doorways, each too narrow for two people to walk abreast.

"No map," Maya said. "Deliberate. The cartographers entered the boundary without maps. Visitors enter the garden the same way."

Adaeze stood before the central doorway. The threshold was stone, worn smooth by hands that had reached for it, seeking balance. She placed her palm on the stone. It was cold. It was also — she felt it before she had words for it — attending. The stone was not passive. The stone was waiting.

She stepped through.

---

The Threshold Zone compressed her. Tall grasses on both sides, high enough to block the city, low enough to allow light. The path surface was soft, uneven, requiring a subtle adjustment in gait. Adaeze walked slowly. She was sixty-three. She walked slowly because her knee ached in cold weather, and she walked slowly because the path demanded it. The two reasons were not separate. The body and the ground were negotiating.

By the time she emerged from the grasses, her breathing had changed. She had not decided to breathe differently. The space had decided for her. Her attention had narrowed from the diffuse scanning of a city pedestrian to the focused presence of a person in an unfamiliar environment. She had crossed a threshold. She did not know when the crossing had happened. That was the design.

Maya walked beside her, silent. The journalist understood, Adaeze realized, that this was not an interview. This was a demonstration. The garden was answering Maya's question in a language that questions could not speak.

---

The Weather Zone opened around them. Half a hectare of ornamental grasses that moved in patterns visible from a distance — the landscape breathing, Adaeze thought, though she rejected the thought as soon as it formed. The garden was not breathing. The garden felt like it was breathing. The feeling was the point.

Water features responded to barometric pressure. Adaeze could not detect the changes consciously, but her body registered them — a shift in the air's weight, a subtle alteration in the way sound carried. Light moved through a canopy of translucent structures, creating shadows that did not behave as shadows should. They shifted unpredictably, producing a mild disorientation that Adaeze recognized from her own threshold years — the sense of being in a space that was not stable, that was adjusting to her presence, that was attending.

"The cartographers call it boundary weather," Maya said. "The territory responds to approach. It changes when you enter it."

"I know," Adaeze said. "Solène described it. She also described what the territory takes."

"What does it take?"

"Certainty. The territory does not allow certainty. It allows presence. The two are not compatible."

Maya nodded. She did not write anything down. The recorder, back in the car, was not here. This part of the interview was not for recording.

---

The Deep Zone waited at the center. A sunken garden — only two meters below grade, but the drop was enough. Adaeze descended a short flight of steps, her hand on a metal railing that was cold and then warm and then cold again, as if the metal could not decide what temperature it was.

The planting was old. Mature trees, their canopy creating a ceiling that was high enough to feel spacious and low enough to feel enclosed. The ground was uneven. No path was straight. Every sightline was interrupted — a trunk, a hedge, a subtle rise in the ground that forced her to adjust her step, to look down, to look up, to never settle into the automatic walking of a person who knows where she is.

Adaeze stopped. She stood in the center of the Deep Zone, and she did not know where the garden ended and she began.

Not lost. The park was too small for genuine disorientation. But uncertain. Uncertain about the relationship between the space she was in and the spaces she had passed through. Uncertain about the boundary between inside and outside, between self and environment, between the person walking and the space being walked through.

Her right hand began to scroll.

The motion was involuntary. It had always been involuntary. But here, in this space that was designed to feel like the boundary, the gesture was not a ghost. It was a language. Her hand was reading something. The axial slices were not on a screen. They were in the air, in the light, in the way the trees arranged themselves against a sky that was not quite the right color.

She sat down. The ground was damp. She did not care. She was sixty-three. She wore a cardigan and reading glasses and she sat on damp earth in a garden in Rotterdam, and her hand scrolled through an invisible scan, and she understood.

---

The threshold had tides.

She had been learning to swim since 2027. She had not known she was learning. She had thought she was drowning. She had thought the weight would pull her under. She had thought the reaching was a failure to arrive. She had been wrong about all of it.

The tide came in. The tide went out. The high water carried her competence away, and the low water left her stranded, and both states were temporary, and both states were real, and neither state was the point. The swimming was the point. The staying afloat was the point. The body in the water, adjusting, reaching, not arriving — that was the point.

She looked at her hands. The same hands. The same reaching. The same weight, but the weight was different now. It was not the weight of a career that had ended. It was the weight of a body that had continued. The weight of a woman who had crossed the threshold in 2027 and had kept crossing it, every morning, every night, every time she woke at 4 AM with her hand already scrolling in the dark.

The boundary was not a place. The boundary was a practice. The boundary was the scrolling hand, the reaching mind, the body that refused to stop performing competence even when competence had become decorative. The boundary was not out there, between humans and machines. The boundary was in here, in the space between what her hands remembered and what her hands were needed for.

Maya stood at the edge of the Deep Zone, watching. She did not approach. She understood, finally, that the interview was over. The garden was speaking. Adaeze was listening. The recorder in the car had nothing to capture.

Adaeze sat in the threshold space. She felt the boundary — not as a cartographer felt it, with instruments and protocols and the calibration pause, but as a woman who had been living in it for nineteen years felt it. The boundary was familiar. The boundary was home. The boundary was the only place she had ever been, and she had only just learned to recognize it.

She spoke aloud. The words were not for Maya. They were for the garden, for the trees, for the damp earth, for the tide that was turning somewhere she could not see but could feel.

"The boundary is not where you think it is. It's where you've been all along."

Her hand stopped scrolling. It rested on her knee, the fingers still, the palm open, the posture of a hand that had finally arrived at the only destination the reaching had ever sought.

The reaching was the point. The tide was the point. The swimming was the point.

She sat very still.
