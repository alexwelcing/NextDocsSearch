# Part II: The Wilderness

## Chapter 6: The Translation Garden

The garden was on Markveien, in a former bookshop that had closed in 2026 when the last independent publisher in Oslo went digital-only. Maren had bought the space with the money from her last professional translation contract — a novel she had rendered into Norwegian with the kind of precision that the machine now achieved in seconds, and she had taken the fee not as payment for the work but as severance from the profession that had made her. The bookshop had smelled of mildew and old glue. The shelves were still mounted on the walls, empty, their brackets rusting slightly where the paint had chipped. The floorboards creaked in a pattern she had learned to read: the third board from the window groaned when someone approached the table, the seventh squeaked near the stove, the twelfth — directly beneath the long wooden table — sang a small note of protest when more than six people sat down at once.

She had not removed the shelves. She had filled them with dictionaries. Not the sleek digital dictionaries that the machine used, the ones that held every word in every language in a database that weighed nothing. Physical dictionaries. Paper. Ink. The kind that smelled like a library and weighed like a brick and had to be carried in two hands. Norwegian-English. English-French. French-Arabic. Arabic-Spanish. Spanish-Japanese. Japanese-Norwegian. A chain of languages linked by human effort, each dictionary a bridge built by someone who had spent years deciding whether *melankoli* was closer to *melancholy* or *spleen* or something that had no English word at all.

The dictionaries were not for reference. They were for weight. They held the shelves to the wall. They held the room to the earth. They were the ballast that kept the garden from floating away into the digital air where translation had become weightless, effortless, free.

The room smelled of coffee grounds and old paper and the particular green scent of plants that grew in northern light — rosemary, thyme, a small lemon tree that Maren had nursed through three winters and that had produced, last autumn, a single lemon the size of a walnut. The lemon sat on the windowsill, yellowing, never picked, a monument to persistence in a climate that did not want it. The stove in the corner was a wood-burning affair, black iron, salvaged from a farmhouse in Telemark, and it gave off a heat that was different from the heat of radiators — a dry, snapping heat that made the air smell of pine resin and made the floorboards expand, changing the creak pattern so that the third board groaned differently in winter than in summer.

It was Tuesday. The garden was open. The regulars were already there — Ingrid in her chair by the stove, her dictionary open to a page she could not read without her glasses, which she refused to wear because she said they made her look like a librarian, which she had been and did not want to be reminded of. Park in the corner, his laptop closed on the table in front of him, a cup of tea cooling untouched, his eyes moving across the room with the particular attention of someone who had built the systems that made the garden necessary and was now trying to understand what he had destroyed. Elin at the far end of the table, her hair the color of a sunset, her pen moving in a notebook she would not show anyone, translating something from Japanese, a language she had started learning six months ago and spoke with the reckless confidence of someone who did not yet know what she did not know.

Maren moved among them. She was not a host. She was a gardener. She tended the space the way she tended the rosemary — with attention, with patience, with the understanding that growth could not be forced. She brought coffee. She opened windows when the stove made the room too warm. She closed them when the Oslo rain began. She said very little. The garden did not need her words. It needed her presence.

---

Maren was planting rosemary when the bell rang.

The bell was a small brass thing she had found in a flea market in Grünerløkka, the kind of bell that had once hung on shop doors in the last century, before sensors and chimes and the smooth electronic tones that announced entry now. This bell had a voice — a small, bright clang that carried the particular timbre of brass struck by brass, a sound that woke the room and made everyone look up.

She was on her knees beside the planter that ran along the front window, her hands in the soil, when the bell rang. She did not stand immediately. She finished pressing the soil around the rosemary cutting — a sprig from her mother's garden in Ulvik, carried on the bus in a damp paper towel — and she lifted the handful of earth to her nose, not smelling but feeling the weight of what would grow. The soil press. The translator's habit transferred. At forty-two she had done this with paper, rubbing a sheet between thumb and forefinger before placing a word. At forty-seven she did it with earth, testing the medium before placing the root.

Then she stood. Her knees ached. Her back ached. The gardener's body was different from the translator's body — wider in the hands, slower in the rising, more willing to ache and keep working. She wiped her palms on her wool trousers and walked to the door.

Henrik was standing on the step. He was eighty-six, or perhaps eighty-seven — Maren had never been certain of his age, and Henrik had never offered it. He had translated legal documents for forty years, contracts and statutes and the small print that made the world legible to itself. He had come to the garden since it opened, five years ago, arriving every Tuesday with a text he had chosen and a thermos of coffee that he shared with whoever was there. He translated slowly, with the particular slowness of someone who had spent his professional life ensuring that no word could be misunderstood, and in the garden that slowness was not a flaw. It was the value.

But today he looked different. He was thinner. The coat that had fit him in October hung from his shoulders like a curtain. His face was the color of old paper, the kind of paper that yellows at the edges first, and his hands — the hands that had held forty years of legal documents with the steady grip of someone who knew that a misplaced comma could cost a fortune — were shaking.

"Henrik," Maren said.

"Maren." His voice was thin. The thinness of a voice that had lost the breath that gave it body. "I have brought something."

"Come in."

He stepped inside. The bell clanged behind him. He walked to the table — the long wooden table, scarred by decades of use, surrounded by twelve mismatched chairs that Maren had collected from estate sales and closing cafés — and he sat in his usual chair, the third from the window, the one with the blue upholstery worn soft at the elbow. He placed a folder on the table. The folder was manila, the kind that legal documents came in, and it was thin. One page, maybe two.

"I am dying," Henrik said.

Maren stood very still. She had known, looking at him on the step, but the saying of it was different from the seeing. The saying made it real in a way that his thinness and his yellowing and his shaking hands had not. She walked to the table. She sat across from him, in the chair with the red cushion, the one that faced the window.

"I know," she said. She did not say she was sorry. She had learned, in five years of the garden, that sorry was not always the right word. Sometimes the right word was *I know*. Sometimes the right word was silence.

"I have one sentence left," Henrik said. He opened the folder. Inside was a single sheet of paper, printed in the dense, formal typeface of a legal document. "I have been saving it. For forty years. I translated it when I was forty-six, and I knew, even then, that it was the best thing I would ever do. I have never shown it to anyone. I have never published it. I have kept it in this folder, in my desk, and I have looked at it once a year, on my birthday, and I have known that the machine could translate it better. Faster. More accurately. But the machine could not translate it *this way*."

He pushed the paper across the table. Maren did not pick it up. She looked at Henrik. His eyes were dry. He was not crying. He was past crying. He was in the place where crying had already happened, where the grief had been absorbed into the body and become something else — a lightness, a clarity, the particular focus of someone who had nothing left to lose.

"What is it?" she asked.

"A will," Henrik said. "From 1847. A farmer in Telemark. He had nothing. A house, some land, a cow. He left the cow to his daughter. He left the land to his son. And he left a sentence to his wife. The sentence is why I kept the document. The sentence is why I became a translator."

Maren picked up the paper. She read the Norwegian first — the original, in the cramped handwriting of a man who had learned to write late in life, the letters uneven, the grammar approximate, the meaning urgent. Then she read Henrik's translation. The English was not elegant. It was not fluent. It was —

She read it again.

*To my wife, who has borne the weight of my silence for thirty years, I leave the sound of my voice. Not the words. The sound. The way I said her name when I still knew how to say it.*

Maren looked up. Henrik was watching her. His hands were folded on the table, the fingers interlaced, the grip loose. He was holding his own hands the way you hold something fragile that you are about to set down.

"The machine would translate it correctly," Henrik said. "It would say: 'To my wife, who has endured my silence for thirty years, I bequeath the sound of my voice.' It would be accurate. It would be legal. It would be *fine*. But it would not be this. This translation is wrong. 'Borne the weight' is not the same as 'endured.' 'Still knew how to say it' is not the same as 'when I was able to speak.' The machine would smooth the edges. The machine would make it *good*. And in making it good, it would make it nothing."

He stopped. His breath was shallow. The sound of it in the quiet room was like paper rustling.

"I kept this sentence for forty years," he said. "I kept it because it was the only thing I ever made that the machine could not make better. Not because I was better than the machine. Because I was worse. Because my translation is awkward and uneven and full of mistakes that a professional translator would correct. Because I was a legal translator, Maren. I translated contracts. I was not a poet. I was not an artist. I was a man who made language precise, and this sentence is imprecise, and the imprecision is the point. The machine cannot be imprecise. The machine cannot be awkward. The machine cannot be *bad* in the way that makes something true."

Maren looked at the paper. She looked at Henrik. She looked at the garden around them — the shelves of dictionaries, the creaking floorboards, the window that let in the gray Oslo light, the rosemary she had just planted in the planter by the door.

"Will you read it?" she asked. "To the garden?"

"I cannot read it myself." Henrik's voice was smaller now. "My voice is gone. The breath is gone. I can speak, but I cannot *say* it. The saying requires more than speaking. It requires — "

He stopped. His hand rose from the table, fingers slightly curled, reaching toward the paper. The pause-and-reach. The gesture Maren knew from her own body, the hand extending toward empty space as if the right word were something you could pluck from the air.

"It requires a voice that shakes," he said. "I no longer have a voice that shakes."

Maren took the paper. She stood. She walked to the window and pressed her palm against the frame — the window touch, now the garden door touch, the press of her palm against wood before she turned to face the room. The garden was filling. It was Tuesday, and the regulars were arriving. Ingrid was in her usual chair, the one by the stove, her eighty-one-year-old hands already turning the pages of a dictionary she could not read without glasses. Park was in the corner, the Korean software engineer who had built machine translation systems and now came to the garden like a clockmaker visiting a museum of gears, his laptop closed, his eyes open. Elin was at the far end of the table, sixteen, her hair dyed the color of a sunset, her pen already moving across a page she would not show anyone.

Maren held the paper. She looked at Henrik. He nodded.

She read the sentence aloud.

Her voice shook. She had not intended it to shake. She had intended to read clearly, precisely, the way she had read translations for twenty years. But the sentence was not a translation. It was a will. It was a man's last attempt to give his wife the sound of a voice that had gone silent. And Maren's voice shook because the shaking was the point. The shaking was the proof that a real person was speaking. The machine could translate the sentence. The machine could not speak it with a voice that shook.

She finished. The room was silent. Not the silence of a room where nothing is happening — the silence of a room where something has happened and no one knows what to say. Ingrid's hands were still on the dictionary. Park's eyes were closed. Elin's pen had stopped moving.

Henrik stood. He walked to the door. He did not look back. The bell clanged as he left, a small bright sound that hung in the air after he was gone, and Maren stood at the window with the paper in her hand and watched him walk down Markveien, his coat hanging from his shoulders, his gait unsteady, his hand rising once to touch the air as if he were reaching for a sentence he had already given away.

She never saw him again.

Nana Rose arrived at four.

She was not a regular. She was a force of nature, a woman who appeared without announcement, who told stories in a voice that filled the garden like weather, who left as suddenly as she came, leaving behind a silence that felt like the aftermath of a storm. She was seventy-three, Nigerian-British, with the bearing of someone who had spent her life speaking to rooms that did not expect to be spoken to. She had been a teacher, a librarian, a storyteller at a children's hospital, and now she was simply Nana Rose — a woman who told stories that were full of errors, and the errors were the value.

She came through the door with a child on each hand. The children were not hers. They were children from the neighborhood, children whose parents worked late, children who had nowhere to go between school and dinner. Nana Rose collected them the way some people collected stamps — casually, with pleasure, without organization. Today she had two: a boy of maybe seven, with the serious face of a child who had already learned to be careful, and a girl of five, with hair in braids that stuck out at angles, the braids of a child who had not sat still for the finishing.

"Maren," Nana Rose said. Her voice was the voice of a woman who had never needed a microphone. "I have a story. The children need a story. The garden needs a story."

"The garden always needs a story," Maren said.

Nana Rose sat in the largest chair, the one with the green velvet that Maren had found in a closing hotel in Majorstuen. The children sat on the floor at her feet, the boy cross-legged, the girl with her legs splayed, her dress pooling around her like a puddle of color. The regulars — Ingrid, Park, Elin, and the others who had arrived in the afternoon — turned their chairs toward Nana Rose. The garden became an audience. The dictionaries on the shelves seemed to lean forward, as if they too wanted to hear.

"Once," Nana Rose said, "there was a dragon who ate riddles."

She paused. The pause was not for effect. It was for breath. Nana Rose had asthma, and her stories came in paragraphs separated by the wheeze of her inhaler. The inhaler was part of the performance. The children waited. The adults waited. The garden waited.

"The dragon lived in a cave full of books. Not ordinary books. Books that had never been written. Books that existed only in the space between the writer's mind and the reader's eye. The dragon ate the riddles in these books. It ate them because riddles were the only thing that tasted like meaning. Everything else — the descriptions, the dialogues, the endings — tasted like paper. But riddles tasted like *understanding*. They tasted like the moment when you finally see what the words were pointing at."

The girl on the floor giggled. The boy shushed her. Nana Rose continued.

"One day, a child came to the cave. A child who could not read. The child said to the dragon: 'I have a riddle for you. What has no voice but speaks, no hands but writes, no eyes but sees?'"

Nana Rose paused again. The inhaler wheezed. She looked at the children. The girl's face was scrunched in concentration, her braids sticking out at angles, her small body leaning forward as if the answer were something she could catch if she moved fast enough. The boy was thinking, his brow furrowed, his lips moving silently, his hands clasped in his lap the way children clasp their hands when they are trying to be good. Ingrid had put down her dictionary. Park had opened his eyes. Elin's pen had stopped moving. The room held its breath.

"The dragon thought," Nana Rose said. "The dragon thought for a hundred years, because dragons live a long time and a hundred years is not very long for a dragon. And then the dragon said: 'I do not know. I have eaten ten thousand riddles, and I do not know the answer to this one.'"

"What was the answer?" the girl asked. Her voice was urgent, the urgency of a child who cannot wait for the ending.

"The child said: 'The answer is a story. A story has no voice but speaks when someone reads it aloud. A story has no hands but writes itself in the reader's mind. A story has no eyes but sees the reader, because the reader is changed by the story, and the story knows it.'"

Nana Rose stopped. She looked around the garden. Her eyes moved from face to face — Ingrid, Park, Elin, Maren, the others — and her gaze was the gaze of a teacher who had spent her life watching children understand. Maren felt the gaze touch her like a hand, a brief pressure, an acknowledgment.

"But here is the thing," she said. "The dragon had eaten the riddle wrong. The child had said: 'What has no voice but speaks, no hands but writes, no eyes but sees?' And the dragon had heard: 'What has no voice but speaks, no hands but writes, no *I* but sees?' The dragon had eaten the word 'eyes' as the word 'I.' Because the dragon was a machine, and machines eat words without tasting them."

The children laughed. The adults laughed. The laughter was not at the dragon. It was at the error. The error was the point. The girl on the floor was laughing so hard she had fallen backward, her dress spread around her, her braids bouncing. The boy was laughing too, the serious face cracked open, the careful child released into joy. Ingrid was laughing, her eighty-one-year-old shoulders shaking, her hands clasped over her mouth as if she were trying to hold the laughter in and failing. Park was laughing, the engineer who had built the machine that made the garden necessary, laughing at the machine's mistake, laughing at the error that turned a dragon into a person.

Maren laughed too. Her laughter came from deep in her chest, from a place she had not felt in years — the place where translation had once lived, before it became verification, before it became the 8% that the machine left for her to polish. The laughter was not about the dragon. It was about the recognition. The recognition that the error was not a flaw. The error was a door. The error was the way in.

"And the dragon," Nana Rose continued, "the dragon who had eaten ten thousand riddles and never made a mistake, the dragon who was perfect and precise and always correct — the dragon had made an error. And the error was beautiful. Because the error meant the dragon was not a dragon. The error meant the dragon was a person. And only people make errors that turn 'eyes' into 'I' and make the riddle mean something new."

She stopped. The room was silent. Then the girl on the floor said, "Nana Rose, you made a mistake."

"Did I?" Nana Rose asked.

"You said the dragon ate the riddle. But dragons don't eat riddles. Dragons eat princesses."

Nana Rose smiled. The smile was wide, the smile of a woman who had been waiting for this exact objection. "Ah," she said. "But this dragon was a vegetarian."

The children shrieked with laughter. The adults laughed too, the kind of laughter that starts in the chest and rises, that fills a room with warmth. Nana Rose stood. She walked to the door, the children following her like ducklings, and the bell clanged as they left, a bright sound that seemed to hang in the air, vibrating with the laughter that had not yet faded.

Maren stood at the window. She pressed her palm against the frame. The garden was quiet now — Ingrid turning pages, Park staring at his closed laptop, Elin writing in her notebook. The dictionaries on the shelves held their weight. The floorboards creaked in their familiar pattern. The rosemary in the planter by the door was already lifting its leaves toward the gray Oslo light.

She understood, standing at the window with her palm against the wood, that the garden was not a place for translation. The garden was a place for presence. The machine could translate Henrik's sentence. The machine could translate Nana Rose's story. The machine could translate everything in the dictionaries on the shelves, every word in every language, faster and better and more accurately than any human in the room.

But the machine could not be Henrik, sitting at the table with his hands folded, his voice thin, his breath shallow as paper, giving away the one sentence he had saved for forty years. The machine could not be Nana Rose, wheezing between paragraphs, making errors that turned "eyes" into "I" and made the children laugh. The machine could not be Ingrid, eighty-one years old, turning the pages of a dictionary with hands that trembled, translating from languages she did not know with the reckless confidence of someone who understood that the trying was the point. The machine could not be Park, the engineer who had built the systems that made the garden necessary, sitting in the corner with his laptop closed, his eyes open, watching the thing he had helped destroy refuse to die. The machine could not be Elin, sixteen, her hair the color of a sunset, writing in a notebook she would not show anyone, making mistakes that no algorithm would make, errors that were the fingerprints of a person.

The garden preserved presence. Not translation. Not product. Not fluency. Presence. The specific, irreducible presence of a human being in a room, making sounds that shook, making errors that meant something new, making the kind of mistakes that only a real person could make.

Maren walked to the table. She picked up Henrik's paper. She folded it carefully, along the crease that had already been there, and she placed it in the drawer beneath the table — the drawer where she kept the things that could not be translated, the things that could only be given. The drawer where she kept the Hauge poem she had translated for her mother, the one the machine had rendered in silk without ache. The drawer where she kept the letter from the journalist at Aftenposten who had called the garden "quaint" and had not understood that quaint was exactly what they were aiming for — the quaintness of something that refused to be useful, the quaintness of failure made beautiful, the quaintness of a room full of people who had come together to be bad at something and had discovered that the badness was the value.

She closed the drawer. She walked to the door. She pressed her palm against the frame — the window touch, the garden door touch, the press of her hand against the boundary between inside and outside — and she looked out at Markveien, at the people walking past, at the gray Oslo light that was not bright enough to make shadows but was bright enough to grow rosemary.

The window was closing. The machine was getting better. Every month, every week, every day, the gap between human and machine fluency narrowed, and the garden became more quaint, more unnecessary, more beautiful in its refusal to be needed.

But the garden was open. The door was open. The reaching continued.

And somewhere, in a folder in a desk that would soon be emptied, a man who had translated legal documents for forty years had left behind a sentence that the machine could translate but could never say. A sentence that required a voice that shook. A sentence that required a breath that was shallow as paper. A sentence that required a person, standing in a room, reading aloud to people who were listening, and in the listening, becoming something they could not have become alone.

The garden was not a translation. The garden was a gathering. And the gathering was the point.
