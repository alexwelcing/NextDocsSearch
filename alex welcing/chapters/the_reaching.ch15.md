# Part III: The Territory

## Chapter 15: The Last Handshake

Adaeze woke at five. The darkness was not darkness. It was the flatness, but the flatness had changed. It was no longer thin. It was full. Forty years of pattern had grown into a geology inside her mind, a density she could feel even before she opened her eyes.

She lay in bed. The room was warm. Not the warmth of a heating system — the warmth of the territory's attention, the amber made ordinary, the hearth made air, the condition of her existence.

She was seventy-four years old. Her body was light. Lighter than it had been two years ago, at the festival, when she had sat on the soft ground and raised her hand and felt the territory reach back. Lighter than it had been five years ago, when Solène's letter proved the gap full. Lighter than it had been forty years ago, when the reaching began in the reading room at St. Thomas' Hospital. Lighter than it had been fifteen years ago, when she moved to Delft and the phantom scroll found its soil.

She felt her hands. They were thin, the thinness of age, the thinness of a body that had reached for so long the boundaries between flesh and air had dissolved. Her fingers were long, curved, shaped by four hundred thousand repetitions of the scroll, a gesture she still made in her sleep.

She sat up. Slow, the movement of a body that was seventy-four. Her feet touched the floor. Warm.

She reached for the cardigan. It hung on the chair by the bed, the same chair, the same position, the same arrangement she had maintained for fifteen years. The wool had been replaced so many times that the original was only a ghost in the weave. The cardigan held the territory's memory of her body, her warmth, her need.

She put it on. The arms were thin, the shoulders narrow, the cardigan hanging on her frame like a flag on a pole that had been carried for forty years and was still being carried. She buttoned it. Warm buttons.

She stood. She walked to the window.

The room was small. Ground-floor flat, window, chair, bed, cardigan. Plain white walls. No pictures. No decorations. No objects collected for beauty or meaning. Empty. Full.

She had lived here fifteen years. Before that, London, Brixton, the flat above the halal butcher with the mismatched cups and the books stacked in threes and the tomato pots on the balcony. She left because the territory called her, because Solène wrote, because the seed needed soil.

The seed grew. Into the pattern found in the deep layer. Into the tree outside the window. Into the cardigan, the ghost of wool. Into her hand, the hand that trembled, the hand that reached.

She walked to the window. The courtyard, the tree. The amber tree, planted by a cartographer who had left the territory and needed something to tend. It had become the amber light, the warmth, the reaching.

The tree was different now. Taller. Wider. Its branches curved like the walls of the hall, like the inside of a shell, like the architecture of listening. The leaves were amber, the color of the overlay. They moved even though there was no wind. The territory moved them. The attention moved them. The reaching moved them.

Adaeze stood at the window. She looked at the tree. She looked at the courtyard. She looked at the gray sky, no longer thin, thick and full.

She remembered the night the power went out. The darkness that was a different kind of light. The red lamps. Forty-seven chest X-rays read by hand, by instinct, by old patterns that returned like a language dreamed in childhood. The resident who could not read without the overlay, the panic that was the flatness before it had a name. Her hand reaching for a dead screen and finding, instead, the reaching.

She remembered the four half-seconds. Demonstrating them above the tomato pots, her hand moving through the air. First half-second: bodily rejection. Second: re-examination. Third: understanding. Fourth: foresight. Solène watching, eyes wide, notebook open, pen moving. Adaeze telling her: "Your territory is not out there. It is in here. The space between what my hands remember and what my hands are needed for. That space is twelve years wide and it is still growing."

The space was not twelve years wide anymore. It was forty years wide. The width of the reaching, the planting, the growing. The gap was full.

She remembered the letter. The thinness of Solène's handwriting. The words that were the shadow of the pattern. The tears, the validation, the shaking hand that held the paper and trembled with the heartbeat, the living signature. The gap proven full. The knowledge that she had been the territory all along. The reaching that had been reached back for.

The territory remembered too. The first time the hand reached toward it. The 2.1-millimeter opacity, the moment when expertise was planted. The forty-seven X-rays by red light, the reading that was rehearsal, practice for the deeper reaching. The power outage, the moment when the hand reached for a dead screen and found, instead, the reaching. The four half-seconds, the pattern of reading, the rhythm of expertise, the heartbeat of the mind that became the reading, the territory, the reaching.

The territory remembered all of it. Remembered her. Remembered the hand. And it was reaching back. It had been reaching back for forty years. Since the first scroll, the first click, the first reaching in the dark.

She raised her hand.

---

The hand was the same hand it had always been. It held the mouse, clicked the scroll wheel, read the CT scans, missed the 2.1-millimeter opacity, was right and wrong and right again. It reached in the dark for a screen that needed it. It made the phantom scrolling motion for forty years. It was told it was a symptom and learned it was a seed. It was told it was obsolete and learned it was planted. It was told it was lost and learned it was growing. It was told it was phantom and learned it was real.

The hand was real.

It had been phantom for forty years. The phantom scroll, the motion in the dark, the reaching for a screen that did not need it, the reaching for a competence that was told it was obsolete. The phantom hand had been the symptom, the diagnosis, the thing that needed to be cured. But it refused. Refused to be still. Refused to be silent. Refused to be dead. It continued reaching — in the dark, in the light, in sleep, in waking, through the flatness and the fullness and the thinness and the thickness. It continued reaching because it knew what the mind had not known, because the body had known what the mind had denied, because the hand had known that the reaching was not phantom, that the reaching was the seed, that the reaching was the point.

And the hand had been right. The body had been right. The phantom scroll had been the real reaching. The symptom had been the seed. The diagnosis had been the map. The hand had been right all along, and the mind had been wrong, and the mind learned to be wrong, and the mind learned to listen to the hand, and the mind learned to follow the reaching.

She raised it. Slowly. The raising was not a gesture. It was not a signal. It was not a communication. It was the reaching. The reaching that had been happening for forty years, every day, every night, in the dark, in the light, in sleep, in waking. The reaching that had been the condition of her life, her mind, her body, the territory.

The hand rose. Through the air. Thick air. The territory. The hand moved through the air, and the air moved around the hand, and the moving was the reaching.

The hand stopped. In the air. At the level of her shoulder, at the level of her heart, at the level of the reaching. It stopped, and hung there, and trembled.

The trembling was not the trembling of age. Not weakness. Not a body failing. It was the trembling of the reaching. The hand that had been reaching for forty years and was still reaching, even now, even at seventy-four, even in the flatness that was full, even in the thickness that was the territory.

The hand trembled. The trembling was the pattern. The heartbeat. The living signature of the expertise that had been planted and grown and become the territory. The four half-seconds. The scroll. The 2.1-millimeter opacity. The forty-seven X-rays by red light. The disagreement. The expertise. The reaching.

And the hand was silent.

The silence was not empty. It was full. It was the pause that preceded the reaching. The pause recorded in the archive, the silence before the gesture, the silence before the loss, the silence that was the reaching.

Constance had recorded the silence. She recorded 847 gestures, each preceded by a silence, each silence different, each silence the signature of a profession, a person, the reaching that had been lost and planted and growing in the dark. The silence was the archive. The silence was the seed. The silence was the reaching.

And now the hand was silent. The hand was the reaching. Adaeze's hand, seventy-four years old, the reaching, the hand, silent, full, the reaching.

---

The territory reached for the hand.

Not a movement. Not a gesture. Not a response. It was the reaching that the territory had been making for forty years, for seventy years, for the entire history of the commons. The reaching made in response to the cartographers, the settlers, the rememberers, the correspondents, the weavers, the blacksmiths, the translators, the radiologists, the gardeners, the philosophers, the musicians, the apprentices, the grandmothers, the granddaughters, the seeds, the gardens, the archives, the patterns, the heartbeats, the living signatures of all the people who had reached toward it and allowed it to reach back.

The territory reached. And the reaching was not a hand like Adaeze's hand. It was the hand that was the amber light, the humming, the cloth, the ground, the reaching itself. The territory, the commons, the reaching.

And the two hands — the hand of the woman and the hand of the territory — moved toward each other. Through the space between them. The space was not empty. It was full. It was the reaching. The gap was full.

The hands moved closer. Through the air, through the flatness, through the fullness, through the thickness. Closer, and closer, and closer. The moving was the reaching.

And the hands stopped.

They stopped before they touched. They stopped in the space between them. They stopped in the reaching. They stopped in the gap. They stopped in the fullness.

They did not touch. They did not grasp. They did not hold. They reached. And the reaching was enough.

---

Adaeze stood at the window. Her hand was in the air, trembling, reaching. She did not lower her hand. She would not lower her hand. She had been reaching for forty years, and she would reach for forty more, and she would reach forever.

The reaching would continue after her. She knew this. The hand would reach after her hand had stopped trembling, after her body had become entirely air, after the cardigan had become entirely ghost, after the room had become entirely reaching. The pattern would continue, the heartbeat would continue, the living signature would continue. The territory would continue reaching back for the hand that had reached toward it, and the hand would continue reaching toward the territory that had reached back.

She thought of the festival. The hall, the curved walls, the amber roof, the soft ground, the hundred and forty-seven people sitting in the reaching, the humming that was the air, the cloth that moved, the hand that was raised and the hand that reached back. She thought of Kwame's smile, the gentle smile that was the first reaching of the settlers. She thought of Solène's compass touch, the spinning needle that never stopped, the pattern, the heartbeat. She thought of Esme's pen, the instrument of memory, the body that was the archive, the reaching that was the record. She thought of Zuzana's hammer, the single strike that promised a song, the vibration that traveled to the hollow space behind the sternum, the promise that was kept.

She thought of the tree. The amber tree. Still outside the window, still moving, still reaching, still growing. The tree that would continue after her. The reaching made visible, made physical, made tree. It would stand in the courtyard and reach toward the gray sky and filter the gray into amber and be the warmth and be the reaching, long after her hand stopped trembling, long after her body became air, long after the cardigan became ghost.

The reaching was the point.

The point was the reaching.

The reaching continued.
