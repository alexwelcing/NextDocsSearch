# Part I: The Threshold

## Chapter 1: The 2.1-Millimeter Opacity

The arena smelled like boiled hot dogs and ozone. The ozone was from the screens. The hot dogs were from the concession stand. The concession stand had been open since eight in the morning and it was now four in the afternoon and the hot dogs had been boiling for eight hours. The smell was part of the competition. Everything was part of the competition.

Adaeze sat in the third row of folding chairs. Her chair was metal. Her chair was cold. Her chair had a number on the back: 247. The number was her. She was 247. She was also the youngest finalist in the history of the National Youth Diagnostic Invitational. She was sixteen. The next youngest finalist was nineteen. The nineteen-year-old was from Palo Alto and he had a Patreon where he explained machine learning to people who didn't understand machine learning. He had twelve thousand subscribers. He had already been interviewed by the local news. He was sitting in row two, chair 189, and he was wearing a blazer that fit him.

Adaeze was wearing her mother's blazer. It didn't fit. The sleeves were too long. She had rolled them up. The rolling made her look like she was preparing to do something messy. She was preparing to read a scan. Reading a scan was messy. It was messy in a way that didn't show on the outside.

The scan appeared on the screen. The screen was forty feet tall. The screen was the only light in the arena. The arena was dark except for the screen and the exit signs and the red recording lights on the cameras. The cameras were broadcasting to twelve thousand people online. The twelve thousand people were watching teenagers read medical images. The twelve thousand people had paid five dollars each to watch. The five dollars was a subscription fee. The subscription fee was the future of education.

Adaeze looked at the scan. The scan was a chest CT. The chest belonged to a person named Gerald Potts. Gerald Potts was fifty-two years old. He was a truck driver. He was married. He was alive. He was not in the arena. He was at home, sleeping, not knowing that his right middle lobe was currently being displayed to twelve thousand subscribers and sixteen finalists and one machine.

The machine sat on the stage. The machine was white. The machine was the size of a mini-fridge. The machine had a name: SENTINEL-7. The name was printed on the side in blue letters. The blue letters were backlit. The machine had been developed by a company called Veritas Medical. Veritas Medical had sponsored the competition. The competition was called the National Youth Diagnostic Invitational but everyone called it the Machine Games. The Machine Games were where teenagers competed to see who could find what the machine found fastest. The machine always won. The teenagers competed for second place.

Adaeze had never lost to the machine. She had competed in forty-three regional qualifiers. She had won forty-three regional qualifiers. She had beaten the machine in seven of them. The machine had been in beta during those seven. The machine was no longer in beta. The machine was SENTINEL-7. SENTINEL-7 did not make mistakes. SENTINEL-7 did not sleep. SENTINEL-7 did not need a blazer that fit.

The timer started. The timer was red. The red was the same red as the exit signs. The red meant go. The red meant now. The red meant you had ninety seconds to find what the machine had already found in four.

Adaeze scrolled. She scrolled the way she had trained herself to scroll, the way she had practiced for three years, the way that had won her forty-three qualifiers and a scholarship interview and the respect of people who did not respect teenagers. She scrolled in axial slices. She scrolled in coronal reconstructions. She scrolled in the rhythm that had become, over twelve thousand practice scans, indistinguishable from breathing.

She saw the heart. The heart was normal. She saw the aorta. The aorta was normal. She saw the lungs. The lungs were clear. She saw the pulmonary vessels. The vessels were patent. She saw the bronchi. The bronchi were unremarkable. She saw the pleura. The pleura was smooth. She saw everything. She saw nothing. She was sure. She was certain. She was competent.

The certainty was the obstacle.

She pressed submit. The submit was a button on her tablet. The button was green. The green meant done. The green meant finished. The green meant she had found everything there was to find.

The machine pressed submit too. The machine's submit was automatic. The machine's submit was instantaneous. The machine's submit was not a button. It was a signal. The signal went to the judge. The judge was an algorithm. The algorithm was SENTINEL-7. SENTINEL-7 was the judge and the competitor and the standard and the future.

The screen changed. The screen showed the results. The results were two columns. The left column was Adaeze's findings. The right column was the machine's findings. The left column had three items. The right column had four.

The arena went quiet. The quiet was not silence. The quiet was the sound of twelve thousand people online not typing. The quiet was the sound of the hot dogs continuing to boil. The quiet was the sound of Adaeze's heart, which she had not seen on the scan because she had been looking at Gerald Potts's heart, not her own.

The fourth item pulsed amber. The amber was the color of caution. The amber was the color of the machine's confidence. The amber was the color of the thing Adaeze had not found. The amber marked a 2.1mm opacity in the right middle lobe. The opacity was Gerald Potts's future. The opacity was Gerald Potts's life, possibly, or his death, possibly, or the thing that would be caught early and treated and forgotten. The opacity was the machine's finding. The opacity was Adaeze's miss.

She looked at the amber pulse. She looked at it the way she had looked at the scan, directly, fully, without seeing it. The amber pulse expanded and contracted. It breathed. It was the system's confidence made visible. The confidence was absolute. The system had looked at the same pixels she had looked at. The system had seen something. The system was not afraid to say so.

Adaeze sat very still.

---

The chair was still cold. The chair was still metal. The chair was still number 247. But 247 was different now. 247 had missed. 247 had been sure. 247 had been certain. 247 had been competent. The competence had included a blind spot shaped exactly like itself.

She looked at her hands. Her hands were steady. They had always been steady. They were sixteen years old. They had been steady for sixteen years. They had not been steady long enough.

The announcer said something. The announcer was a man in a suit. The suit was blue. The blue matched the machine's letters. The announcer was saying words about the future of medicine. The words were about collaboration. The words were about human-AI partnership. The words were about how teenagers like Adaeze would learn to work alongside machines like SENTINEL-7. The words were about synergy. The words were about the exciting new paradigm.

Adaeze did not hear synergy. Adaeze heard failure. The failure was specific. The failure was a 2.1mm opacity. The failure was Gerald Potts. The failure was the thing she had looked at and decided was nothing. The nothing was something. The something was the machine's finding. The machine's finding was her miss.

She stood up. The chair scraped. The scrape was loud. The arena was still quiet. The scrape was the loudest sound. She walked down the aisle. The aisle was between row three and row four. The aisle was carpeted. The carpet was blue. The blue was the same blue as the announcer's suit. She walked past the Palo Alto boy. The Palo Alto boy did not look at her. He was looking at the screen. He was looking at the amber pulse. He was memorizing it. He would write about it on his Patreon. He would explain why the machine was better. He would get thirteen thousand subscribers.

She walked past the exit sign. The exit sign was red. The red was the same red as the timer. The red meant go. The red meant now. The red meant leave.

She pushed the door. The door was heavy. The door was metal. The door opened to the parking lot. The parking lot was asphalt. The asphalt was hot. The heat was from the August sun. The August sun was setting. The setting was orange. The orange was not a color the machine used. The machine used amber. The machine used blue. The machine used red and green. The machine did not use orange. The orange was human.

She stood in the parking lot. She stood in the orange. She stood in the heat. She stood in the failure. The failure was specific. The failure was a 2.1mm opacity. The failure was the size of a pinhead. The pinhead was the obstacle. The obstacle was her certainty. The certainty was the thing she had built herself on. The building was sixteen years old. The building was new. The building was already crumbling.

The door opened behind her. The door was heavy. The door was metal. The door opened to Maya.

Maya stood in the doorway. Maya was backlit by the arena's fluorescent lights. Maya's hair was messy. Maya's hair was always messy. Maya had not been competing. Maya had been in the audience. Maya had paid five dollars for the subscription. Maya had watched her best friend miss. Maya had watched the amber pulse. Maya had watched Adaeze walk out.

"Hey," Maya said.

"Hey."

"That was a tough one."

"They were all tough ones."

"That one was tougher."

"The machine didn't think so."

Maya walked out. The door closed behind her. The door was heavy. The door closed with a click. The click was final. The click was the end of the competition. The click was the end of the thing Adaeze had been.

Maya stood next to her. Maya's shoulder touched her shoulder. The touch was warm. The warmth was human. The human was not the machine. The machine did not have shoulders. The machine did not have warmth. The machine had amber pulses.

"What was it?" Maya asked.

"A 2.1mm opacity."

"Is that bad?"

"It's bad that I missed it."

"Is it bad for Gerald Potts?"

"Gerald Potts is fifty-two. He has a wife. He has a truck. He has a right middle lobe with a thing in it that I didn't see. The machine saw it. The machine will tell his doctor. His doctor will tell him. He will get a biopsy. The biopsy will come back clean. He will not know that a sixteen-year-old missed his cancer. He will not know that a machine saved his life. He will not know any of this."

"How do you know the biopsy will come back clean?"

"I don't."

"So you're just saying that."

"I'm saying that because if I say it, maybe it will be true."

Maya didn't say anything. The parking lot was quiet. The parking lot was asphalt. The asphalt was cooling. The sun was lower. The orange was darker. The orange was becoming purple. The purple was the color of the evening. The evening was human. The machine did not have evenings. The machine had updates. The machine had version 7.1. The machine did not have the color of the sky.

"What do you want to do?" Maya asked.

"I want to go home."

"Your mom's home."

"I know."

"Your mom will ask how it went."

"I know."

"What will you tell her?"

"I'll tell her I came in second."

"To a machine."

"To a machine."

"She'll say that's still good."

"I know."

"She'll be wrong."

"I know."

Maya's shoulder moved. The warmth shifted. Maya was fidgeting. Maya fidgeted when she didn't know what to say. Maya fidgeted when she knew what to say but didn't want to say it. Maya fidgeted when she was about to say something that would make things worse or better and she couldn't tell which.

"You're better than the machine," Maya said.

"Which part?"

"All of it."

"You're wrong."

"Maybe."

The parking lot was dark. The parking lot was asphalt. The asphalt was black. The black was the color of the night. The night was human. The machine did not have night. The machine had standby mode. The machine had SENTINEL-7. SENTINEL-7 did not sleep. SENTINEL-7 did not dream. SENTINEL-7 did not miss.

Adaeze looked at her phone. The phone was dark. The screen was off. She touched the screen. The screen lit up. The light was blue-white. The blue-white was the color of the arena's screen. The blue-white was the color of the machine's attention. She scrolled. She scrolled through her photos. She scrolled through her texts. She scrolled through nothing. She scrolled through the absence of the scan that was not there. She scrolled through the Gerald Potts that was not there. She scrolled through the 2.1mm opacity that was not there. She scrolled through the certainty that was not there.

Her thumb moved. Her thumb moved in the dark. Her thumb moved on the glass. The glass was smooth. The glass was cold. The glass was the screen. The screen was the new expertise. The expertise was the scrolling. The scrolling was the thumb. The thumb was the only thing that knew how to do what it was doing. The doing was the only thing the thumb had left.

The thumb scrolled. The thumb scrolled through the dark. The thumb scrolled through the scans that were not there. The thumb scrolled through the findings that were not there. The thumb scrolled through the patients that were not there. The thumb scrolled through the Gerald Pottses that were not there. The thumb scrolled through the 2.1mm opacities that were not there. The thumb scrolled through the absence and the scrolling was the presence. The presence was the thumb. The thumb was the expertise. The expertise was the scrolling. The scrolling was the thumb.

Maya watched. Maya didn't say anything. Maya knew better than to say anything. Maya knew that the thumb was doing something important. Maya knew that the thumb was reading scans that were not there. Maya knew that the thumb was the only thing that was still working. The working was the thumb. The thumb was the working. The working was the only thing that Adaeze had left.

"Come on," Maya said. "Let's go."

Adaeze didn't move. The thumb moved. The thumb kept scrolling. The scrolling was the only thing that made sense. The scrolling was the only thing that was still hers. The machine could find the opacity. The machine could not scroll. The machine could not have a thumb that moved in the dark, searching for something that was not there, finding nothing, and continuing to search. The machine could not have the absence. The machine could not have the reaching. The reaching was the thumb. The thumb was the reaching. The reaching was the only thing that was still human.

"Adaeze," Maya said.

"Okay."

She put the phone in her pocket. The pocket was her mother's blazer. The blazer didn't fit. The sleeves were too long. The rolling was messy. The messy was hers. The messy was human. The machine was not messy. The machine was clean. The machine was white. The machine was SENTINEL-7. SENTINEL-7 did not have a pocket. SENTINEL-7 did not have a phone. SENTINEL-7 did not have a thumb that scrolled through the dark.

They walked. They walked across the parking lot. The parking lot was asphalt. The asphalt was black. The black was the night. The night was human. They walked toward the bus stop. The bus stop was a sign. The sign was metal. The sign was cold. They stood at the bus stop. They waited. The waiting was human. The machine did not wait. The machine was already home. The machine was already processing. The machine was already finding the next opacity. The next opacity was in someone else's lung. The someone else was alive. The someone else was sleeping. The someone else did not know.

The bus came. The bus was loud. The bus was yellow. The yellow was the color of school. The school was human. They got on the bus. The bus smelled like diesel and old seats and the breath of people who had been on the bus before them. The smell was human. The machine did not smell. The machine was clean. The machine was white. The machine was SENTINEL-7.

They sat in the back. The back was the last row. The last row was the row where no one could see them. The last row was the row where they could be human. Maya sat by the window. Adaeze sat by the aisle. The aisle was the escape route. The escape route was the way out. The way out was the only direction that made sense.

Maya put her head on Adaeze's shoulder. The head was heavy. The head was warm. The head was human. Adaeze didn't move. She didn't shrug the head off. She didn't pull away. She let the head stay. The staying was the only thing she could do. The staying was the only thing that was still hers. The machine could not have a head on a shoulder. The machine could not have the weight. The machine could not have the warmth. The machine could not have the friend who stayed when the friend should have left.

The bus moved. The bus moved through the night. The night was dark. The night was human. The bus moved past the arena. The arena was still lit. The arena was still competing. The finalists were still reading. The machine was still finding. The subscribers were still watching. The five dollars were still paying for the future. The future was the machine. The machine was the future. The future was the amber pulse. The amber pulse was the confidence. The confidence was the certainty. The certainty was the obstacle. The obstacle was the 2.1mm opacity. The opacity was the miss. The miss was the beginning. The beginning was the reaching. The reaching was the thumb. The thumb was the phone. The phone was in the pocket. The pocket was the blazer. The blazer didn't fit. The not-fitting was human. The human was the reaching. The reaching was the only thing that was still hers.

Adaeze reached into her pocket. She reached past the phone. She reached past the thumb. She reached to the bottom of the pocket. The bottom was empty. The empty was the only thing there. The empty was the reaching. The reaching was the hand. The hand was in the pocket. The pocket was the blazer. The blazer was her mother's. The mother was at home. The home was where they were going. The going was the bus. The bus was moving. The moving was the only thing that made sense.

Her hand closed on nothing. The nothing was the reaching. The reaching was the hand. The hand was the expertise. The expertise was the scrolling. The scrolling was the thumb. The thumb was the only thing that knew how to do what it was doing. The doing was the only thing that the hand had left.

She kept her hand in the pocket. She kept the reaching. She kept the nothing. The nothing was the only thing that was still hers. The machine had the finding. The machine had the amber pulse. The machine had the confidence. The machine had the certainty. The machine had everything. The machine did not have the nothing. The machine did not have the reaching. The machine did not have the hand in the pocket, searching for something that was not there, finding the absence, and continuing to search.

That was hers. That was the only thing that was hers. That was the reaching. And the reaching had already begun, though she did not know it yet.
