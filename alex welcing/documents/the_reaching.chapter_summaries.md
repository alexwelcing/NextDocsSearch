## Ch 01: The 2.1-Millimeter Opacity (2,559w)
**Summary**: Adaeze Nwosu, a radiologist with twenty-three years of experience, reads a chest CT at 2 AM and misses a 2.1mm pulmonary nodule that an AI overlay flags instantly. Her certainty — the very competence she had built her career on — becomes the obstacle. In the aftermath, she begins reading with the AI overlay always on, becoming a judge of disagreements rather than a primary reader. She meets resident Priya Chakrabarti, who articulates the fear that their corrections are becoming training data for their replacement. Adaeze's right hand begins making phantom scrolling motions in the dark, the gesture of a competence that no longer needs her body. The chapter ends with her voice memo: "I don't know what I am now" — and the deeper knowledge that her hand, reaching in the dark for a scan that needs it, has already begun the reaching.
**Characters**: Adaeze Nwosu: from confident primary reader to curatorial adjudicator; her hand becomes a phantom gesture of obsolete competence. Priya Chakrabarti: sharp resident who articulates the training-data paradox.
**Environment**: St. Thomas' Hospital reading room, 2 AM — stale coffee, ozone, hand sanitizer, the blue-white light of monitors. Adaeze's flat in Brixton.
**Hook-out**: Adaeze's hand makes the scrolling motion in the dark, reaching for a competence that will never reach back the same way.
**Foreshadowing**: F01 PLANTED (the scrolling hand — phantom gesture of radiologist expertise). F02 PLANTED (the 2.1mm opacity / planted expertise — expertise never lost, only planted, to be paid off in Chapter 23).
**Threads**: adaeze-arc: ACTIVE — from competence to uncertainty. threshold-moment: ACTIVE — the 2.1mm opacity as the moment AI surpassed human expertise. the-reaching: ACTIVE — the hand reaching in the dark.

## Ch 02: The Polish (2,179w)
**Summary**: Maren Solberg, one of the world's finest literary translators, receives a machine translation of a Norwegian novel so good she changes only eight percent of the text. She visits the MT team in Oslo and discovers her own published translations are in the training corpus — her excellence was the fuel for her own obsolescence. On a flight home, she tests the machine against an Olav H. Hauge poem and confirms: the machine is correct, elegant, and entirely without ache. She retreats to her mother's house in Ulvik, where she realizes the machine can translate but cannot gather. She opens the Translation Garden in a former bookshop on Markveien — a space for deliberate, unpaid, communal translation where the failure is the value. A journalist calls it "quaint." Maren understands: the garden is resistance.
**Characters**: Maren Solberg: from solitary expert to communal gatherer; her excellence becomes the seed of her obsolescence and the foundation of her resistance. Maren's mother: practical, sees the truth before Maren does.

**Hook-out**: The garden is open. The window is closing. The reaching has begun.
