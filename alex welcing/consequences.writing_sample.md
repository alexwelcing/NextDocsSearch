# Consequences — Writing Sample (Revised with Character)

> **Scene**: Adaeze's bedroom, 10 PM on a Tuesday. She's supposed to be asleep. Her best friend Maya snuck in through the window — which is ridiculous because the front door works fine, but Maya insists the window is "more cinematic." Adaeze is staring at a kidney. Maya is trying to make her feel better about being worse than a machine.

---

The screen filled the room with blue light. It was the only light. Maya sat cross-legged on the bed, her taco socks pulled up to her knees, and she was looking at the same kidney Adaeze had been staring at for forty minutes.

"You're still on that thing?"

"Yeah."

"It's been an hour."

"Forty minutes." Adaeze didn't look up. "Forty minutes is an hour in friend time, I get it."

Maya picked up the mug from the nightstand. It was her mug. She drank without asking. She never asked. "What are you even looking at?"

"Mrs. Patterson's kidney."

"Is she okay?"

"The machine says she's fine."

Maya leaned closer to the screen. The red box was small and sharp, a little warning flag around a shadow the machine had found in four seconds. Maya squinted at it. She thought it was cool. She always thought the machine was cool.

"So why do you look like someone killed your plant?"

"I don't have a plant."

"You had a succulent."

"It died."

"Everything you have dies?"

Adaeze looked at the empty mug in Maya's hand. The mug was Maya's. The coffee inside had been hers too, until Maya drank it without asking. The coffee was gone. The asking was gone.

"Everything I have dies or gets automated."

"That's dramatic."

"It's accurate."

Maya set the mug down. She moved closer. The bed creaked, which it always did, and she smelled like vanilla from the cheap shampoo she bought at the pharmacy because she liked the bottle. Maya's hair was not cheap. Maya was not cheap. She was free and warm and she didn't know what the machine was doing to Adaeze because she couldn't see it.

"What did you find?"

"Nothing. I looked at that kidney for forty minutes and I saw a kidney. A normal kidney. Mrs. Patterson's normal, perfectly healthy kidney that the machine saw a shadow in."

"A shadow is bad?"

"Finding the shadow is good. The machine found it. The machine found it and explained it in a paragraph. A better paragraph than anything I wrote in three years."

"Four seconds is fast."

"Four seconds is also faster than forty minutes. I know that's not what you meant. I know you meant something supportive. But the math doesn't care what you meant."

Maya looked at the screen. She didn't touch it. She never touched it. She was afraid of it the way she was afraid of the math teacher, which was a respectful fear, a fear that kept you alive.

"The machine is just a tool."

"That's what the brochure says."

"You're not a tool."

"I'm a tool that's slower than the other tool."

"You're not a tool." Maya said it again, louder, like repetition made it true. "You said that already."

The room went quiet. The bed didn't creak. The screen hummed, low and steady, the sound of the machine being awake. The machine didn't sleep. It didn't need Maya's vanilla shampoo or her taco socks or her voice repeating things that weren't true. It just hummed and found shadows and wrote better paragraphs.

Maya's face was soft. It was always soft. She looked at the red box and she saw something cool. She saw the future. Adaeze looked at the red box and saw the end of something she had spent three years building, three years of learning to read the scan, to find the anomaly, to be the one who saw what others missed, and then the machine didn't miss anything.

"I should go," Maya said.

"Okay."

"I have homework."

"Okay."

"Stop saying okay."

"Okay."

Maya got up. She walked to the door and she stopped there, not turning around, and she said the thing she always said when she didn't know what else to say: "You're better than the machine."

"Which part?"

"All of it."

"You're wrong."

"Maybe."

Maya left. The door stayed open. Adaeze looked at the open door and then at the screen and then at the empty mug on the nightstand. Maya's mug. Maya's coffee. The coffee was gone. The asking was gone. The door was open.

The screen was still blue. The red box was still there. Mrs. Patterson's shadow, Mrs. Patterson's future, the thing the machine had found while Adaeze was still looking at a normal kidney. She had found nothing. Nothing was what she had.

She picked up the mug. It was empty. She put it down. She didn't wash it. She looked at the screen. The blue was flat. The flat didn't care. She didn't care either, except that she cared about not caring, which was the only thing she could still feel, and even that was getting smaller, like the shadow on the screen, like the machine's attention, flat and blue and already moving on to the next kidney, the next shadow, the next thing she wouldn't see.

---

**Word count**: ~800  
**Scene type**: Dialogue + interiority + visual close-up  
**Core dynamic**: Friend tries to comfort; Adaeze can't be comforted because the gap between them is the machine  
**Character work**: Maya's taco socks, vanilla pharmacy shampoo, window-sneaking habits, and the coffee she never asks for. Adaeze's dead succulent, her "everything dies or gets automated" line, the specific way she won't wash the mug. These are people, not positions.
