# Humor Mechanics Guide

How to engineer comedy in prose: irony, juxtaposition, timing, and satire.

## 1. The Irony Hierarchy

### 1.1 Dramatic Irony (The Audience Knows More)
**Setup**: The reader knows something the character does not.
**Sustain**: The gap between reader knowledge and character action creates tension.
**Payoff**: The character discovers the truth, or the gap produces comedy through the character's confident misunderstanding.

**Comedy-specific rule**: The reader must feel safe. In comic dramatic irony, the character's misunderstanding must not cause real harm — the harm must be to pride, dignity, or social standing, not to body or soul. If harm is possible, the comedy curdles into dread.

**Timing**: The longer the gap is sustained, the funnier the payoff — up to a point. Beyond 2–3 scenes, the reader may become frustrated rather than amused.

### 1.2 Situational Irony (Expectation vs. Outcome)
**Setup**: Establish a clear expectation through context, convention, or character confidence.
**Subversion**: The outcome violates the expectation in a way that is logical but unforeseen.
**Scale**: The gap between expectation and outcome must be proportional to the buildup. A small setup + huge subversion = randomness. A huge setup + small subversion = anticlimax.

### 1.3 Verbal Irony (Saying One Thing, Meaning Another)
**Modes**:
- **Understatement**: "The room was a bit warm" (the room is on fire)
- **Overstatement**: "I have not slept in forty years" (I am tired)
- **Deadpan**: The narrator reports the absurd as if it were mundane
- **Sarcasm**: The speaker intends the listener to understand the opposite

**Voice rule**: Define which mode is native to this voice. A voice that defaults to deadpan will use sarcasm only under extreme stress. A voice that defaults to sarcasm will never use deadpan — it would read as confusion.

## 2. Juxtaposition Engineering

### 2.1 Register Collision
Place two incompatible registers in contact:
- Scientific + Vulgar: "The coefficient of friction was approximately zero, so he fell on his ass."
- Bureaucratic + Intimate: "Regarding your request for emotional intimacy, please submit Form 7-B."
- Sacred + Profane: "The angel spoke unto him, saying, 'Dude, chill.'"

**Rule**: The collision must be abrupt. One sentence ends in one register; the next sentence begins in another. No transition, no warning.

### 2.2 Scale Shift
Move suddenly between cosmic and domestic:
- "The universe expanded at 67.4 km/s/Mpc, and she forgot to buy milk."
- "The supernova was visible from their kitchen window, where Kevin was doing the dishes."

**Rule**: The small thing must be rendered with the same gravity as the large thing. The comedy lives in the equality of attention, not in the dismissal of the small thing.

### 2.3 Anachronism
Insert a modern object or concept into a historical (or futuristic) context, or vice versa:
- "The medieval knight checked his phone."
- "The AI paused its calculations to consider whether it was happy."

**Rule**: Anachronism works only if the rest of the context is consistent. One anachronism is comedy. Two is confusion. Three is a different genre.

## 3. Comic Timing in Prose

### 3.1 The Sentence-Level Beat
The final word of a sentence is the punchline position. Structure sentences so that the unexpected word lands last.

**Bad**: "He was surprised to find a chicken in the cockpit, which was unusual." (punchline in middle, dilution at end)
**Good**: "The cockpit was clean, organized, and contained a chicken." (punchline at end)

### 3.2 The Paragraph-Level Beat
Comedy often requires a three-part structure:
1. Setup (establish normal)
2. Escalation (introduce weird)
3. Payoff (the weird becomes normal, or the normal collapses)

**Example**:
> She made tea. This was her ritual: the kettle, the cup, the waiting. She had made tea in this kitchen for forty years, and the ritual had never changed. The kettle whistled. She poured. She added milk. She looked out the window. The garden was gone. In its place, a single, very large button.

### 3.3 The Scene-Level Beat
Comic scenes need a turning point — the moment when the scene shifts from setup to payoff. Before the turning point, the reader should feel the tension of something about to go wrong. After the turning point, the reader should feel relief that the wrong thing is happening.

**Turning point markers**: A silence that lasts too long. A door that opens unexpectedly. A sentence that begins normally and ends with the wrong word.

## 4. Satire as Surgery

### 4.1 The Target
Satire requires a specific target, not a general attitude. "Society" is not a target. "The HR department's mandatory fun initiative" is a target.

### 4.2 The Blade
Satire is not cruelty. It is accuracy. The sharpest satire is the satire that the target would recognize as true but would never admit.

**Rule**: Describe the target's behavior in its own language. Let the target's words indict the target. Do not add commentary.

### 4.3 The Escape Hatch
Even satire needs humanity. After the indictment, allow one small moment that makes the target human:
- The bureaucrat who designed the absurd form goes home and feeds his cat.
- The CEO who destroyed the company cries at a commercial.

Without the escape hatch, satire becomes propaganda. With it, satire becomes art.

## 5. Running Gags & Callbacks

### 5.1 The Rule of Three
- First mention: noticed, not commented on
- Second mention: noticed, briefly acknowledged
- Third mention: payoff — the pattern becomes the joke

### 5.2 The Callback
A detail from an earlier scene returns in a later scene, transformed:
- The broken watch from Chapter 1 is found in a drawer in Chapter 10, still broken.
- The joke from the first meeting becomes the last line of the book.

**Rule**: Callbacks must be earned. The detail must have been established without signaling that it would return. If the reader feels manipulated, the callback fails.

## 6. The Anti-Comedy List

These patterns destroy comedy:
- **Explaining the joke**: "This was funny because..." → never
- **Undercutting with self-awareness**: "I know this is a cliche, but..." → the voice must commit fully
- **Winking at the reader**: Any gesture that says "we both know this is fiction" → breaks the comic contract
- **Cruelty without context**: Comedy that hurts a character the reader cares about, without the character's consent → becomes tragedy or meanness
- **Randomness**: Comedy that violates internal logic without purpose → becomes nonsense

## 7. Humor Audit Checklist

For any comic passage:
- [ ] The irony is clear and the gap is sustained
- [ ] The juxtaposition is abrupt and registers collide
- [ ] The punchline lands at the end of the sentence/paragraph/scene
- [ ] The satire targets something specific and uses the target's own language
- [ ] There is an escape hatch — humanity after the indictment
- [ ] The running gag follows the rule of three
- [ ] The callback is earned, not forced
- [ ] No anti-comedy patterns appear
