# Visual Sensibility Guide

How to encode a visual mindset into prose craft rules — so the writing sees like a camera, lights like a cinematographer, and textures like a sculptor.

## 1. Camera Distance & Movement

### 1.1 The Default Lens
Every voice has a default distance from its subject. This is not arbitrary — it is a moral and emotional position.

| Distance | Effect | Example Voices |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| **Extreme wide** (landscape, city) | Character is small, context is vast | Epic, historical, fatalistic |
| **Wide** (full figure, room) | Character in environment | Observational, social, architectural |
| **Medium** (waist up, two-shot) | Character in relationship | Dramatic, dialogue-heavy, theatrical |
| **Close** (face, hands, object) | Detail is emotion | Intimate, tactile, forensic |
| **Macro** (texture, pore, grain) | The world becomes abstract | Obsessive, analytical, ecstatic |

**Rule**: Define the default distance for this voice. Then define the exceptions — when does the voice pull back? When does it push in?

### 1.2 Zoom Rules

**Pull back** (wide → close):
- When the character is overwhelmed by context
- When the reader needs to see the pattern the character cannot see
- When the scene is ending and the voice wants to leave the reader with the environment, not the person

**Push in** (close → wide):
- When the character discovers something that matters only to them
- When the reader needs to feel the texture of a moment
- When the scene is beginning and the voice wants to establish intimacy before context

**Rule**: Zooms should be motivated by emotion, not by information. If the voice is pushing in to show the reader a clue, the zoom is mechanical. If the voice is pushing in because the character is suddenly afraid of what they see, the zoom is alive.

### 1.3 POV Stability

**Fixed POV**: The camera never moves. The voice stays with one character, one distance, one angle. Creates claustrophobia and intensity.

**Drifting POV**: The camera moves between characters, distances, angles. Creates omniscience and irony. Risk: can feel like head-hopping if not controlled.

**Unreliable POV**: The camera shows what the character sees, but the character sees wrong. Creates dramatic irony and tension.

**Rule**: Define the POV stability in the blueprint. If drifting, define the rules of drift: when can the voice move? How is the transition marked?

## 2. Light & Color

### 2.1 Light Quality

**High-key**: Bright, even, few shadows. Suggests safety, clarity, exposure, but also surveillance and absence of privacy.

**Low-key**: Dark, directional, deep shadows. Suggests danger, mystery, intimacy, but also oppression and concealment.

**Practical**: Light from visible sources (lamp, candle, screen, fire). Suggests realism, domesticity, warmth, but also limitation and confinement.

**Motivated**: Light that seems to come from a source but is actually emotional. The room is bright because the character is happy, not because the sun is shining. Suggests subjectivity, unreliability, poetry.

**Chiaroscuro**: Extreme contrast between light and dark. Suggests moral ambiguity, duality, tension. One half of the face lit, one half in shadow.

**Rule**: Define the default light quality for this voice. Then define the emotional triggers for light changes.

### 2.2 Color Vocabulary

A voice's color vocabulary is not a list of favorite colors. It is a grammar of meaning.

**Example color grammar**:
- **Amber**: warmth, memory, the past, hospital corridors at 2 AM
- **Blue**: cold, clinical, distance, screens, the future
- **Green**: growth, the territory, the uncanny, nature reclaiming
- **White**: blankness, the map, possibility, absence
- **Gold**: transcendence, the far shore, late light, the end

**Rule**: Define 3–5 colors that carry meaning in this voice's world. For each, define: the literal referent, the emotional resonance, and the narrative function.

**Forbidden colors**: Colors that this voice cannot use because they are alien to its worldview. (e.g., a voice built on fluorescent and amber would never use "pastel." A voice built on earth and stone would never use "neon." A voice built on saturation and contrast would never use "beige.")

### 2.3 Light as Emotion

Light should signal interior state before the character names it.

**Patterns**:
- The light dims as the character's hope dims
- The light sharpens as the character's attention sharpens
- The light flickers as the character's certainty flickers
- The light becomes flat as the character becomes numb

**Rule**: The light change must happen before or simultaneous with the emotional change, never after. If the character feels sad and then the light dims, the light is illustration. If the light dims and then the character notices they feel sad, the light is causation.

## 3. Texture & Motion

### 3.1 Surface Attention

A voice that attends to texture is a voice that believes the world is knowable through the body.

**Texture hierarchy**: Define which textures this voice notices and which it ignores.

**Example**:
- A medical voice: smooth, cool, resistant, yielding, wet, dry
- A culinary voice: crisp, soft, melting, gritty, viscous, flaky
- A mechanical voice: polished, scored, oiled, rusted, tight, loose

**Rule**: The texture vocabulary should be consistent with the voice's source domain. A voice that draws metaphors from cooking should notice food textures in non-food scenes. A voice that draws metaphors from medicine should notice body textures in non-body scenes.

### 3.2 Motion Description

How does the voice describe bodies and objects in motion?

**Verbs of motion**: Define the preferred motion verbs.
- **Staccato**: jerk, snap, flick, twitch, jolt
- **Flowing**: drift, glide, slide, spill, pool
- **Weighted**: drop, sag, settle, press, lean
- **Precise**: align, adjust, target, track, measure

**Rule**: The motion verbs should match the voice's emotional rhythm. A staccato voice uses staccato verbs. A flowing voice uses flowing verbs. A voice that shifts between rhythms should shift between verb sets.

### 3.3 Stillness

Stillness is not the absence of motion. It is the presence of tension.

**How to render stillness**:
- Through the body: held breath, locked posture, fixed gaze
- Through the environment: frozen water, stopped clock, silent machine
- Through the sentence: long sentence without verbs, or short sentences without objects
- Through repetition: the same word, the same image, the same rhythm — until the reader feels the strain

**Rule**: Stillness must be earned by prior motion. A still scene after a still scene is flat. A still scene after motion is the held note.

## 4. The Sound-Off Test

### 4.1 Visual Carrying Capacity

Every scene must be comprehensible with dialogue removed. This does not mean every scene must be silent. It means the visual information must be sufficient to understand the emotional event.

**How to test**:
- Write the scene
- Remove all dialogue
- Read the remaining description
- Can you tell what happened? Can you tell how the characters felt?
- If not, the scene is under-visualized

### 4.2 Visual Motifs

A visual motif is an image that recurs across the narrative, carrying thematic weight without dialogue.

**Examples**:
- Hands: scrolling, hammering, catching, reaching, paring, extending
- Light: amber pulse, monitor glow, candle flame, fog glow, golden afternoon
- Water: rain, tide, fog, mist, tea, coffee, tears, the sea
- Walls: the hospital corridor, the blank map, the wall in Delft, the boundary

**Rule**: A motif must appear at least three times to be recognized as a motif. The first appearance is information. The second is pattern. The third is meaning.

### 4.3 The Cinematic Paragraph

A paragraph that is composed like a shot:
- One dominant visual element
- One dominant emotional element
- One motion (or one stillness)
- One light quality
- One texture

**Example**:
> The reading room smelled of ozone and old coffee. Adaeze sat in the blue-white light, her hand on the trackpad, scrolling. The monitor flickered. She stopped. The amber pulse breathed. She did not breathe. The 2.1mm opacity was there, and she had not seen it.

Visual elements: reading room, monitor, amber pulse, opacity
Emotional element: failure, doubt
Motion: scrolling → stopped → breathed → did not breathe
Light: blue-white, amber
Texture: ozone, coffee, the smooth plastic of the trackpad (implied)

## 5. Visual Sensibility Audit Checklist

For any passage:
- [ ] The default camera distance is established and consistent
- [ ] Zooms are motivated by emotion, not information
- [ ] POV is stable or drifts according to defined rules
- [ ] Light quality signals interior state before the character names it
- [ ] Color vocabulary is consistent with the voice's grammar
- [ ] Textures match the voice's source domain
- [ ] Motion verbs match the voice's emotional rhythm
- [ ] Stillness is earned by prior motion
- [ ] The scene passes the sound-off test
- [ ] Visual motifs recur with increasing weight
- [ ] At least one paragraph reads like a cinematic shot
