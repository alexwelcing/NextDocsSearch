# Diction Register Guide

How to construct a diction register that is concrete, enforceable, and distinctive.

## 1. The Vocabulary Triad

Every voice has three layers of vocabulary:

### 1.1 Native Vocabulary (60–70% of word choices)
Words this voice uses without thinking. They are the grammatical furniture — the words that appear in every passage regardless of scene type.

**How to identify them**:
- Read 5 passages from the source (or the writer's prior work)
- List the 50 most frequent nouns, verbs, and adjectives
- Mark the ones that appear in every passage regardless of topic
- These are the native vocabulary

**Example (Hemingway-adjacent)**: water, light, hand, wall, drink, wait, know, good, clean, dark

### 1.2 Borrowed Vocabulary (15–25%)
Words this voice reaches for when the subject demands it. A medical voice borrows culinary terms for a surgery scene. A pastoral voice borrows technical terms for a machine.

**Rule**: Borrowed vocabulary must be earned. It appears when the scene's subject demands it, not when the writer wants to show off.

### 1.3 Forbidden Vocabulary (the negative space)
Words this voice would never use. Not just cliches — words that are fundamentally alien to the voice's worldview.

**Example**: A voice built on tactile precision would never use "landscape" as a metaphor for emotional state. A voice built on irony would never use "heartfelt" sincerely.

## 2. Register Shifts as Signals

Register shifts are not errors — they are communicative tools. Define when and how they happen.

### 2.1 The Elevation Rule
When does the voice rise to a higher register?
- **Triggers**: death, revelation, love confessed, violence witnessed
- **How**: simpler words → more precise words; concrete → abstract (briefly); short sentences → longer, balanced clauses
- **Duration**: 1–3 sentences, then fall back

### 2.2 The Collapse Rule
When does the voice fall to a lower register?
- **Triggers**: exhaustion, intimacy, defeat, humor
- **How**: Latinate words → Anglo-Saxon words; abstract → concrete; complex sentences → fragments
- **Duration**: sustained until the scene's emotional temperature changes

### 2.3 The Code-Switch Rule
When does the voice borrow from another dialect or register?
- **Triggers**: character dialogue, reported speech, cultural reference
- **How**: brief, bounded, marked by quotation or context
- **Danger**: code-switching without marking is voice drift

## 3. Metaphor Discipline

### 3.1 Source Domain Consistency
A voice that draws metaphors from cooking should not suddenly produce a metaphor from astrophysics unless the scene is about astrophysics. The source domain is part of the voice's identity.

### 3.2 Metaphor Density Formula
- **Sparse**: 1 metaphor per 300+ words (Hemingway, Carver)
- **Moderate**: 1 metaphor per 100–150 words (most literary fiction)
- **Dense**: 1 metaphor per 50–75 words (poetic prose, lyrical voices)
- **Saturated**: multiple metaphors per sentence (rare, high-risk)

### 3.3 The Dead Metaphor Ban
List the specific metaphors this voice must never use. Dead metaphors are not cliches — they are metaphors that have become so common they are no longer perceived as figurative.

**Universal dead metaphors**: journey, battle, landscape, tapestry, rollercoaster, roller-coaster, thread, weave, fabric, depth, height, weight, light, dark

**Note**: Some voices can revive dead metaphors by literalizing them. A voice obsessed with actual weaving can use "thread" literally. A voice that has never woven must avoid it.

## 4. The Anti-AI Lexicon

These words and phrases are statistical markers of AI-generated prose. A distinctive voice must either ban them entirely or redefine them so completely they become unrecognizable.

### 4.1 High-Frequency AI Verbs
delve, embark, navigate, underscore, highlight, underscore, reveal, demonstrate, serve as a testament to, stand as a testament to, underscore the importance of

### 4.2 High-Frequency AI Nouns
tapestry, landscape, journey, narrative, paradigm, framework, discourse, realm, sphere, domain, continuum, dichotomy, synergy, nuance, complexity, multifaceted, multifacetedness, intricacies, implications, underpinnings, ramifications

### 4.3 High-Frequency AI Adjectives
compelling, poignant, evocative, profound, vibrant, rich, diverse, robust, dynamic, nuanced, multifaceted, intricate, complex, nuanced, layered, textured, resonant, evocative, immersive, captivating, poignant, evocative, profound, compelling

### 4.4 AI Sentence Frames
- "In the [adjective] landscape of [noun], [subject] [verb]..."
- "[Subject] serves as a testament to [abstract noun]."
- "The [noun] underscores the [noun] of [noun]."
- "At the intersection of [noun] and [noun], [subject] [verb]..."
- "It is important to note that [obvious statement]."
- "This is not just [concrete thing]; it is [abstract thing]."

### 4.5 The Fix
For every AI word the voice needs, replace it with:
- A more specific synonym
- A concrete image
- A phrase from the voice's native vocabulary
- A metaphor from the voice's source domain

## 5. Diction Audit Checklist

When reviewing a passage for diction compliance:
- [ ] No AI vocabulary words appear
- [ ] Native vocabulary words appear naturally, not forced
- [ ] Borrowed vocabulary is appropriate to the scene's subject
- [ ] Metaphor density matches the blueprint
- [ ] Register shifts are intentional and marked
- [ ] No dead metaphors unless literalized
- [ ] Sentence frames are original, not AI-patterned
- [ ] Each word earns its place — could any be cut or replaced with a more precise choice?
