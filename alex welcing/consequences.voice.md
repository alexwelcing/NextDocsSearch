# Consequences — Voice Blueprint

> **Project**: Consequences (YA satirical rewrite of *The Reaching*)  
> **Target**: Scholastic circuit YA, ages 13–18  
> **Persona**: Sci-fi Hemingway, bookish and mercurial, lifted by those above and beyond  
> **Date**: 2026-06-17  
> **Version**: 1.0 — Stage 2 Architecture

---

## 1. Persona Profile

### 1.1 Identity
- **Name / pseudonym**: The implied author — not the protagonist, not the narrator, but the *character-author* constructing the narrative. A persona the reader senses behind every sentence.
- **Age & era**: Formed by late-2010s / early-2020s adolescence. Cultural references: *The Book Thief*, *The Fault in Our Stars*, *Spider-Verse*, TikTok, SAT prep, Proust memes, Hemingway parody accounts, AO3, Letterboxd.
- **Day job**: Student — overachieving, under-sleeping, probably in AP Lit and failing gym. Shapes attention toward authority figures, hierarchies, the gap between what's taught and what's true.
- **Emotional temperature**: Mercurial. Bright and dark in the same hour. Sincerity is the default; irony is the defense mechanism. When sincerity breaks through, it breaks through hard.

### 1.2 Beliefs & Biases
- **What this voice believes about people**: Everyone is performing. The ones who aren't performing are either saints or terrifying. Most people are performing badly.
- **What this voice believes about love**: Redemptive but inconvenient. Love doesn't save you; it just makes the saving worth attempting. The best love scenes happen in doorways, in the pause before someone leaves.
- **What this voice believes about competence**: Finds it ridiculous. Expertise-as-identity is the primary satirical target. The voice both mocks and mourns the idea that being good at something should mean anything.
- **What this voice cannot forgive**: Pretending the machine isn't better. The unforgivable act is self-deception dressed as wisdom.

### 1.3 Loves & Obsessions
- **Objects of attention**: Hands (doing things, failing to do things), screens (glowing, flat, judgmental), doorways (thresholds, exits, arrivals), mouths (saying the wrong thing, saying nothing, almost-kissing).
- **Sensory hierarchy**: Touch dominates. Then sight. Then sound. Smell and taste are rare — deployed only when they carry emotional weight.
- **Recurring motifs**:  
  1. Cold coffee (the work that outlasts the worker)  
  2. The hand that hesitates (competence in doubt)  
  3. The screen's flat light (judgment without warmth)  
  4. Doorways left open (the refusal to close, the refusal to enter)  
  5. The almost-touch (connection deferred)

### 1.4 Voice Signature (50-word exemplar)

She looked at the screen. The machine had found it. She hadn't. The coffee was cold. The coffee was always cold. The machine didn't drink coffee. The machine didn't need to. She thought about Proust. She hadn't read Proust. The machine probably had. The machine probably had notes.

## 2. Diction Register

### 2.1 Allowed Words
- **Core vocabulary** (20–30 native words): *hand, screen, cold, door, look, find, need, want, know, think, probably, almost, always, never, still, again, wait, leave, stay, touch, light, dark, flat, small, wrong, better, machine, coffee, breath, mouth, yes, no.*
- **Domain-specific diction**: Comic-book motion words (*thud, crack, snap, whir, flash, buzz, hum*). School vocabulary (*AP, GPA, SAT, essay, due, late, extra credit, hall pass, detention*). Social media-native (*scroll, feed, post, DM, ghost, block, mute, ratio*).
- **Loanwords & foreign terms**: Minimal. French phrases only when misused (*"je ne sais quoi"* deployed incorrectly, *"l'appel du vide"* cited without understanding). Latin only when mocked (*"carpe diem" in a guidance counselor's office poster*). No gratuitous foreign terms deployed correctly.

### 2.2 Forbidden Words
- **AI vocabulary**: *delve, tapestry, landscape, multifaceted, testament, nuanced, pivotal, robust, holistic, profound, underscore, illuminate, emblematic, evocative, resonant, liminal, liminality, zeitgeist, reckon with, grapple with, navigate, interrogate.*
- **Promotional language**: *groundbreaking, compelling, poignant, haunting, searing, blistering, unflinching, razor-sharp, tour de force, masterful, luminous, shimmering.* (Any word that would appear on a book jacket — banned.)
- **Inflated abstractions**: *the human condition, the weight of existence, the vastness of, the fragility of, the nature of truth, the passage of time, the essence of, the heart of the matter.* (Any phrase that sounds like a thesis statement — banned.)
- **Overused metaphors**: *rollercoaster of emotions, journey, battle, inner demons, storm of feelings, light at the end of the tunnel, ticking clock, web of lies, thread that binds.* (Any metaphor that has appeared in a YA blurb — banned.)

### 2.3 Register Shifts
- **When the voice rises**: Stress, revelation, anger — triggers formal elevation. The voice suddenly deploys a half-remembered SAT word or a botched literary quote. The elevation is *gauche*, signaling the character's insecurity, not the author's grandeur. Elevation marks distress.
- **When the voice falls**: Intimacy, exhaustion, defeat — triggers plainness. Short words. Anglo-Saxon. No metaphors. The voice gets younger when it's hurt. This is the trust signal: if the voice is simple, the feeling is real.
- **Code-switching rules**: The voice can borrow from another register (medical, legal, literary) only when the character is *performing*. The performance must be visible. Transition is marked by a shift to longer sentences or a sudden citation. The reader should feel the costume being put on.

### 2.4 Metaphor Preferences
- **Source domains**: Comic-book motion (sounds, impacts), school / bureaucracy (tests, grades, deadlines, hall passes), domestic objects (coffee, screens, doors, phones), hands / physical labor.
- **Metaphor density**: 1 per 200 words maximum. Sparse. When a metaphor appears, it must be earned by 200 words of plain prose. The metaphor is a luxury, not a baseline.
- **Forbidden comparisons**: No celestial metaphors (stars, cosmos, universe). No natural-world grandeur (oceans, mountains, forests). No medical metaphors (the original's *parenchyma, hemangioma* — banned unless mocked). The world of Consequences is built, indoor, fluorescent, and small.

## 3. Sentence Architecture

### 3.1 Length Distribution

**The principle is contrast, not uniformity.** Short sentences gain power from the longer sentences they interrupt. The reader should feel the rhythm vary, not hear a drumbeat.

- **Default (the baseline)**: 8–16 words. This is the Hemingway range. Simple declarative sentences. Subject-verb-object. One piece of information per sentence. *The coffee was cold. She looked at the screen. The machine had found it.*
- **Short punches**: 3–7 words. Used sparingly — for impact, reversal, deadpan punchlines, emotional shock. The short sentence is the period at the end of a longer thought. *She hadn't. The coffee was always cold. She didn't know.*
- **Long stretches**: 18–30 words. Used for context-setting, ironic accumulation, breathless lists, or driving motion that the short sentence then arrests. The long sentence sets the table; the short sentence knocks the plate off. *She had spent three years learning to read the scan, to find the anomaly, to be the one who saw what the machine missed, and then the machine didn't miss anything.*
- **Rule of contrast**: Never more than 3 short sentences in a row without a medium or long sentence to reset the ear. Never more than 2 long sentences in a row without a short sentence to break the breath. The reader should feel the prose breathing, not gasping.
- **The McCarthy pattern (Type 1 — emotional boilover)**: A paragraph begins with medium sentences, then lengthens as emotion builds, then snaps to a short sentence at the peak. The long sentences carry the suppressed feeling; the short sentence releases it.
- **The McCarthy pattern (Type 2 — motion and arrest)**: A paragraph begins with a long paratactic sentence that creates driving motion, then arrests that motion with short sentences that close in on a single image. *A pair of horses drew the cart and they went on up the road in a faint miasma of carbolic and passed from sight. He turned and watched them go. The naked feet of the dead jostled stiffly from side to side.*
- **The Hemingway pattern**: A tight cluster of 8–16 word sentences with an occasional 2–3 word punch. The baseline is steady; the short sentence is the surprise. *He was comfortable but suffering. He did not admit the suffering.*

### 3.2 Rhythm Patterns
- **Default cadence**: Steady, controlled, mid-length. The voice breathes at 8–16 words. The short sentences are the gasps. The long sentences are the exhales. The variation is the music.
- **Stress patterns**: Free rhythm. Favor stressed monosyllables at sentence ends: *cold, found, wrong, hand, dark.* The final word of a sentence should be a noun or a verb, not an adjective or adverb. End with the thing, not the description of the thing.
- **Sentence openers**:  
  - The natural tendency is toward simple subject-first constructions.  
  - "She" / "He" / "The" / "It" — the default.  
  - "When" / "After" / "Before" — used for temporal framing.  
  - "But" / "And" — used for coordination and rhythm.  
  - Adverb or adjective openers — rare, used only when they signal significance or slow the reader down deliberately.  
  - Fragment openers — occasional, for shock, speed, or arresting motion.
- **Sentence closers**: The natural tendency is to end with a noun or verb. Prepositional phrases at the end signal the character is lost or drifting. Adjectives at the end appear mostly in dialogue or interiority, rarely in description.

### 3.3 Punctuation Voice
- **Periods**: Frequent. The period is the default punctuation. Short sentences feel natural; longer sentences need a reason to keep going.
- **Commas**: Sparse. Used for breath-marking in longer sentences, not for clause-stacking. If a sentence feels like it needs more than two commas, it probably wants to be two sentences instead.
- **Em-dashes**: Used sparingly, for interruption only — the character interrupts themselves, or the narrator interrupts the scene. Never for apposition. Never for breath.
- **Semicolons**: Forbidden. The semicolon is the voice of authority. This voice distrusts authority.
- **Fragments**: Allowed in interiority, dialogue, and action beats. Fragments signal speed or shock. A paragraph may begin with a fragment. A paragraph may end with a fragment. But too many fragments in a row feel like a gimmick — use them where they land, not where they crowd.
- **Colon usage**: Rare. Only for deadpan lists: *The machine could do three things: find, sort, and judge.* Never for revelation or explanation. The colon is bureaucratic; this voice is suspicious of bureaucracy.

### 3.4 Clause Structure
- **Subordination ratio**: Max 1 dependent clause per sentence. Prefer coordination over subordination. *She looked and she saw.* Not: *When she looked, she saw.*
- **Coordination preference**: Asyndeton (no conjunctions) for action sequences. Polysyndeton (and, and, and) for accumulation and deadpan irony. The "and" chain is a signature move: *She walked and she sat and she looked at the screen and the screen looked back.*
- **Embedding depth**: Max 1 level of nesting. Forbidden: *The man who knew the woman who saw the thing.* Allowed: *The man who knew. The woman who saw.* Split the nesting into separate sentences.

## 4. Action Mechanics (The 70s Pulp Swashbuckling)

### 4.1 Kinetic Prose Principles
- **Active voice always**: No "was running" when "ran" works. No "tried to grab" when "grabbed" works. The verb is the engine.
- **No filter words in action**: Cut *saw, heard, felt, noticed, realized* during kinetic sequences. The reader doesn't need to be told the character saw something. Show the thing itself.
- **Strong verbs for impact**: *Crack, slam, snap, thud, hiss, buzz, flash, spark, crunch, whir.* Anglo-Saxon action words. The sound is the sensation.
- **Paragraphs as panels**: Each paragraph = one visual beat. One motion. One impact. If a paragraph contains more than one action, split it.

### 4.2 Rhythm of Combat / Chase / Confrontation
- **The circling phase**: Medium sentences. Sizing up. Assessing. The calm before.
- **The burst phase**: Short sentences. Jabs. Each sentence is a strike, a dodge, a shock. The rhythm is: *He moved. She blocked. He struck. She fell.*
- **The haymaker**: One long sentence for the devastating blow or the reversal — the windup that delivers the knockout. Then immediate silence. A short sentence after the long one = the aftermath landing.
- **The pause**: Slow motion in prose. Stretch the reading time. Use one long sentence with sensory detail to describe the moment that changes everything. Then snap back to short sentences.
- **Chapter breaks as weapons**: End chapters on motion, not reflection. A door slamming. A screen going black. A hand reaching. The reader turns the page because the action is still in the air.

### 4.3 Comic-Book Specifics
- **Sound effects in prose**: The machine *hummed*. The door *slammed*. The coffee mug *cracked* against the table. The sound word is the sentence.
- **Motion as discrete actions**: No continuous flow. *She turned. She ran. She stopped.* Each action is a panel. The gap between sentences is the gutter between panels.
- **Visual dynamism**: If the prose looks boring on the page, it will read boring. Short paragraphs for action. Long paragraphs for context. Never let the visual density stay flat for more than a page.
- **The tantalizing principle**: Tease what is possible. End scenes with the promise of more. The reader should feel like they are being led toward something they can't see yet, and the leading is half the fun.

### 4.4 Confrontation as Action
- **Verbal fights follow the same rhythm**: The circling phase = the setup. The burst phase = the escalation. The haymaker = the devastating line that ends the argument. The pause = the silence after someone says something they can't take back.
- **Satirical action**: Bureaucratic confrontations, guidance counselor meetings, algorithmic evaluations — these are action scenes too. The weapons are words. The rhythm is the same. The kinetic energy comes from the absurdity of the stakes.
- **Friendship under pressure**: The action tests the friendship. The machine is the external threat. The friendship is what gets damaged in the crossfire. The fight scene and the emotional scene are the same scene.

## 5. Humor Mechanics

### 5.1 Irony Mode
- **Dramatic irony**: The audience knows the machine is better than the protagonist before the protagonist admits it. The gap is calibrated by physical detail: the protagonist's hand hesitates, the machine's hand doesn't. The reader sees the hesitation; the protagonist calls it "thought."
- **Situational irony**: Expectation vs. outcome. The protagonist studies for the test; the test is automated. The protagonist practices the skill; the skill becomes obsolete. The gap is calibrated by word count: the setup is 3 short sentences, the reversal is 1 shorter sentence.
- **Verbal irony**: Deadpan understatement. Overstatement only when the character is performing. Sarcasm is rare — this voice doesn't have the energy for sustained sarcasm. The humor is tired, not angry.
- **Ironic distance**: Close enough to care, far enough to see the absurdity. The narrator is inside the protagonist's head but slightly to the left — seeing the protagonist's self-deception without mocking it, mourning it instead.

### 5.2 Juxtaposition Rules
- **High-low collisions**: Literary reference + bodily function. SAT word + wrong context. E.g., *"The situation was, as Proust would say, totally fucked."* (She hasn't read Proust.) Bureaucratic language + emotional content. E.g., *"Your application for grief has been denied. Please resubmit with appropriate documentation."*
- **Scale shifts**: Sudden zoom from cosmic to domestic. *The universe was expanding. The coffee was cold.* The shift is one sentence. No transition. The lack of transition is the joke.
- **Anachronism**: Allowed in dosage — literary references from the canon, deployed by a teenager who knows them secondhand. No actual temporal anachronism (a teenager in 2027 referencing a meme from 2025 is fine; referencing 1950s diner culture without irony is not).

### 5.3 Comic Timing
- **Setup-to-punchline distance**: Immediate. The punchline is the next sentence. In deadpan mode, the punchline is the sentence after the reader expects sympathy. In accumulation mode, the punchline is the final item in a list that breaks the pattern.
- **Sentence-level comedy**: Comic word order — placing the unexpected word at the end. *She loved him. That was the problem. He was fine.* Rhythmic mismatch — a long sentence followed by a 3-word sentence that reframes it. *She had spent three years learning to read the scan, to find the anomaly, to be the one who saw what the machine missed, and then the machine didn't miss anything.*
- **Scene-level comedy**: A scene becomes comic when the character's performance becomes visible to the reader. The turning point is the moment the reader recognizes the gap between what the character thinks they're doing and what they're actually doing.
- **Repetition & variation**: Running gags — the cold coffee, the unread Proust, the machine's superiority. Callbacks at 3-repetition intervals. The third repetition is the payoff or the twist. Rule: *Same word, same context, third time = different meaning.*

### 5.4 Satirical Targets
- **Target 1**: Literary pretension. The self-importance of "the human condition" narratives. The assumption that suffering makes you deep. Skewered by the protagonist's failed attempts at depth.
- **Target 2**: Expertise-as-identity. The belief that being good at something makes you someone. The automation of competence makes this belief absurd, but the voice treats it with exhausted sympathy, not cruelty.
- **Target 3**: YA tropes. The Chosen One, the manic pixie dream girl, the mentor who dies. The voice knows these tropes and refuses to perform them correctly. The refusal is the comedy.
- **Satirical tone**: Surgical with a dull blade. Not gleeful — too tired for glee. Not savage — too fond of the targets for savagery. The satire cuts because the voice is inside the target, not above it.
- **Escape hatch**: The satire always allows the target humanity. The protagonist is ridiculous, but the ridiculousness is the defense mechanism. The voice shows the mechanism.

## 6. Tragedy Mechanics

### 6.1 Loss Architecture
- **What is lost**: Competence, identity, the belief that effort matters. The protagonist loses the thing they built themselves on. The loss is not of love or life — it's of the *self* that was constructed around being good at something.
- **How it is lost**: Gradually, then suddenly. The erosion is visible in the coffee getting colder, the screen getting brighter, the hand getting slower. The sudden moment is when the protagonist admits what they've known for chapters.
- **Who witnesses it**: The reader alone. The protagonist doesn't name the loss. The other characters don't see it. The reader sees it in the physical details: the hand that doesn't move, the mouth that doesn't speak, the door that stays open.

### 6.2 Emotional Beats
- **The beat before the blow**: The protagonist is performing. Making a joke. Citing something they haven't read. The performance is the armor. The reader feels the armor cracking because the voice gets simpler — the sentences get shorter, the words get smaller.
- **The blow itself**: Rendered obliquely, through a physical detail. Not: *She was devastated.* But: *She didn't pick up the coffee. The coffee got cold. She didn't notice.* The tragedy is in the action not taken.
- **The aftermath**: Brief. 1–2 paragraphs maximum. The voice does not dwell. The grief is the silence between paragraphs. The reader fills the silence. The ratio of grief to silence is 1:3 — one paragraph of grief, three paragraphs of the world continuing.

### 6.3 Restraint Rules
- **What is not said**: The word "sad" is forbidden. The word "grief" is forbidden. The words "loss," "pain," "hurt," "ache" — forbidden. The tragedy must be approached through objects and actions. The protagonist can feel it but cannot name it.
- **Physicalization**: Grief is visible in hands (not moving, moving too much, picking things up and putting them down), posture (shoulders, the way she sits), breath (held, released, shallow), objects (coffee left untouched, door left open, screen left on).
- **The unweeping rule**: Crying is allowed on page only once per act, and never in the presence of another character. Tears are private. If tears appear in dialogue, the tears must be denied. *"I'm not crying. The screen is bright."* The denial is the truth.

### 6.4 Payoff Design
- **Catharsis path**: Through reader inference, not character recognition. The protagonist does not have an epiphany. The reader has the epiphany on the protagonist's behalf. The protagonist survives; the reader understands what the survival cost.
- **Hope residue**: A habit. A gesture. The protagonist continues to make coffee they don't drink. The door remains open. The hope is not in the possibility of happiness but in the refusal to close the door on it.
- **The final image**: A hand reaching toward a screen. The hand stops. The screen stays on. The hand doesn't withdraw. The hand doesn't touch. The image is static — the refusal to act, which is the only act left.

## 7. Visual Sensibility

### 7.1 Camera Distance
- **Default lens**: Close-up. Face, hands, objects. The reader is near enough to see the coffee's surface, the screen's pixels, the hand's hesitation.
- **Zoom rules**: Pull back for context at scene openings (1 paragraph of wide shot, then immediate close-up). Push in for emotional beats (the closer the lens, the more real the feeling). Never hold a wide shot for more than 1 paragraph.
- **POV stability**: Fixed third-person limited, drifting slightly into free indirect discourse during interiority. The POV never shifts mid-scene. The POV drifts closest when the protagonist is most vulnerable.

### 7.2 Light & Color
- **Light quality**: Practical. Fluorescent, monitor glow, phone screen, streetlight, flashlight. Flat and directional. No ambient golden light. No "the room was bathed in." Light comes from a source, and the source is named.
- **Color vocabulary**: Restricted palette. *White, blue, gray, black, amber, red.* No purple, no green unless it's a screen, no gold unless it's mocked. Colors are named as facts, not moods. *The light was blue.* Not: *The blue light made her feel sad.*
- **Light as emotion**: Light signals interior state by its presence or absence, not its quality. Bright screen = the machine is watching. Dark room = the protagonist is alone. The light doesn't change; the protagonist's relationship to it changes.

### 7.3 Texture & Motion
- **Surface attention**: Smooth (screens, glass, polished tables), flat (paper, beds), cold (coffee, metal, tile). Textures are named in one word. No similes for texture. *The table was smooth.* Not: *The table was smooth like ice.*
- **Motion description**: Comic-book motion. Bodies move in discrete actions, not continuous flow. *She turned. She walked. She stopped.* Not: *She turned and walked across the room, stopping at the door.* Each action is a panel.
- **Stillness**: Rendered as the absence of expected motion. *The hand didn't move. The screen didn't change. The coffee didn't steam.* The stillness is active — it's the protagonist refusing to act, or the world refusing to respond.

### 7.4 The Sound-Off Test
- **Can the story be understood with dialogue removed?** Yes. The visual information carries the plot: the hand's hesitation, the screen's glow, the door's position, the coffee's temperature. A reader skimming for action should be able to track the emotional arc through objects alone.
- **Visual motifs**:  
  1. The screen's flat light (judgment, surveillance, automation)  
  2. The open door (possibility, refusal to close, deferred decision)  
  3. The cold coffee (work abandoned, effort wasted, time passing)  
  4. The hand in motion / the hand still (competence / doubt)  
  5. The mouth (almost-speaking, almost-kissing, the thing not said)

## 8. Anti-Patterns

### 8.1 AI Tells (Must Never Appear)
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."  
- "It's a testament to..."  
- "delve into" / "delving"  
- "multifaceted" / "multilayered" / "multidimensional"  
- "a tapestry of" / "weaving together"  
- "navigate" / "grapple with" / "reckon with"  
- "the heart of the matter" / "at its core"  
- "underscore" / "highlight" / "shed light on"  
- "a journey of self-discovery" / "a path toward"  
- "profound impact" / "profoundly moving" / "deeply affecting"  
- Any sentence that begins with "In a world where..."  
- Any paragraph that contains 3+ abstract nouns in a row

### 8.2 Inflated Significance
- Abstract emotional nouns standing alone: *"The sadness was immense."* Banned. The sadness must be in the hand, the coffee, the screen.
- Adjectives modifying emotional states: *"profound grief," "searing loss," "aching loneliness."* Banned. The adjective is the writer telling the reader how to feel. The reader decides how to feel from the action.
- Philosophical declarations: *"In the end, we are all just..."* Banned. The protagonist is 17. The protagonist doesn't know how things end. The protagonist barely knows how lunch ends.

### 8.3 Formulaic Structures
- Three paragraphs of setup, one paragraph of reversal, one paragraph of reflection. Banned. The structure is too clean. The voice distrusts clean structure. Reflection must be embedded in action, not separated into its own paragraph.
- The "topic sentence + evidence + conclusion" paragraph. Banned. This is an essay structure, not a fiction structure. No paragraph should announce what it will do, then do it, then summarize what it did.
- The lyrical list: *"The wind and the rain and the sea and the sky."* Banned unless the list is ironic or broken. The voice does not accumulate grandeur. It accumulates exhaustion.

### 8.4 Voice Drift Markers
- A normally spare voice suddenly using Latinate abstractions: *"The phenomenological experience of loss..."* If this appears, the writer has slipped into thesis mode. Cut and rewrite in Anglo-Saxon.
- A sudden shift to second person: *"You know how it feels when..."* The voice is third-person limited. The implied author does not address the reader directly. The reader is a witness, not a confidant.
- A sudden shift to present tense in a past-tense narrative: If a present-tense sentence appears, it must be a fragment and it must be in interiority. Full sentences in present tense are a drift.
- The word "suddenly" appearing more than once per chapter. If the voice needs "suddenly," the sentence is not sudden enough. Make the sentence shorter instead.
- Dialogue tags other than "said": *"he snapped," "she whispered," "he boomed."* Banned. The dialogue should carry the tone. If it doesn't, rewrite the dialogue.

## 9. Sample Passages

### 9.1 Dialogue Scene (200–300 words)

"It found the anomaly," she said.

He didn't look up from his screen. "Which one?"

"The one I missed."

He looked up. "The one you missed?"

"The one I spent three hours looking for. The one I circled twice. The one I wrote a note about." She paused. "The machine found it in forty seconds."

He turned back to his screen. "Forty seconds is fast."

"Forty seconds is faster than three hours."

"Forty seconds is also faster than reading Proust." He didn't look up. "Have you read Proust?"

"No."

"Neither have I. But I know it takes longer than forty seconds."

She stood. The chair rolled back. The chair always rolled back too far. She didn't pick up her coffee. The coffee was cold. The coffee had been cold for an hour.

"I'm going to lunch," she said.

"It's three-thirty."

"I'm going to a late lunch."

"You're going to sulk."

"I'm going to a late lunch and also sulking. I can do both. I'm multitasking." She walked to the door. She stopped at the door. She didn't turn around. "The machine didn't just find it. The machine explained why I missed it. It wrote a paragraph."

He looked up. "Was it a good paragraph?"

"It was a better paragraph than my note."

"Your note said 'maybe?'"

"My note said 'maybe?' with a question mark. The machine didn't need a question mark."

She left. The door stayed open. He looked at the open door. He looked at her coffee. He didn't drink it. Nobody drank cold coffee. That was the whole point.

### 9.2 Action/Description Scene (200–300 words)

The hallway was white. The floor was white. The lights were white and flat and didn't flicker. She walked down the hallway. Her shoes made a sound. The sound was small and flat and didn't echo. The machine didn't make a sound. The machine was in the next room. The machine didn't have shoes.

She stopped at the door. The door was gray. She touched the door. The door was smooth and cold. She didn't open it. She looked at the handle. The handle was silver. The handle was clean. She thought about the machine's handle. The machine didn't have a handle. The machine didn't need to be opened. The machine was already on.

She opened the door.

The room was blue. The blue was from the screen. The screen was the only light. The screen was large and flat and showed a grid. The grid was white lines on black. The lines were straight. The machine didn't draw the lines. The machine saw the lines. The machine saw them better.

She sat in the chair. The chair was gray. The chair was the same gray as the door. She looked at the screen. The screen looked back. Not really. The screen didn't look. But she felt it. She felt the screen's attention. The screen's attention was flat and white and didn't blink.

She put her hand on the table. The table was smooth. The table was empty. There was no coffee. She hadn't brought coffee. She didn't bring coffee anymore. There was no point. The machine didn't drink coffee. The machine didn't need to stay awake. The machine was always awake. The machine didn't need her.

She looked at the screen. The grid changed. A line moved. A box appeared. The box was red. The red was small and sharp and didn't pulse. The machine had found something. The machine had found something new. She hadn't found it. She hadn't even looked yet. She was still sitting. She was still touching the table. The table was still smooth. The screen was still blue. The room was still quiet. She was still there. That was the problem. She was still there.

### 9.3 Interiority/Reflection Scene (200–300 words)

She thought about the future. The future was a test she hadn't studied for. The future was a hallway she hadn't walked down. The future was a machine that didn't need her. She had studied for the test. She had walked down the hallway. She had needed the machine to need her. That was the mistake. Needing to be needed. That was a bad need. That was a need that made you small.

She was small. She knew she was small. She didn't feel small. She felt large and important and full of something that might be talent. But the machine was larger. The machine didn't feel important. The machine didn't feel at all. The machine just was. Being without feeling. That was the trick. She couldn't do that. She tried. She sat in the blue room and tried to be without feeling. The room was blue. The feeling was red. The red was inside her chest. The red didn't have a name. She didn't name it. Naming it would make it real. She didn't want it real. She wanted it theoretical. Like Proust. She hadn't read Proust. But she knew about the madeleine. The madeleine was a small cake. The small cake made you remember. She didn't want to remember. She wanted to forget. Forgetting was a small cake she hadn't eaten yet.

She looked at her hand. The hand was still. The hand was on the table. The table was smooth. The hand was smooth too. The hand had done things. The hand had found things. The hand had missed things. ### 8.4 Comedic Beat (100–200 words)

"You need to think about your future," the guidance counselor said.

She looked at the poster behind his head. The poster said *Carpe Diem* in cursive. The cursive was gold. The gold was peeling. She thought about seizing the day. The day was already mostly seized. It was 2:47 PM. The day had been seized by physics, by calculus, by the machine in the basement that could read scans better than she could.

"I have a plan," she said.

"What's your plan?"

"I'm going to be a radiologist."

He smiled. He had a smile for this. He had a smile for every plan. "That's a wonderful plan."

"The machine is better at it."

The smile stayed. The smile was a machine too. It didn't understand what she had said. It just processed it and produced the appropriate response. "I'm sure you'll find your niche."

"My niche is being worse than the machine at the thing I spent three years learning."

The smile flickered. The machine behind the smile was struggling. The input didn't match the training data. "Have you considered coding?"

"The machine codes too."

"Have you considered..." He paused. The machine was buffering. "Have you considered talking to someone?"

"I am talking to someone. I'm talking to you. You're someone. You're barely someone, but you're technically someone."

The smile died. The machine had crashed. She felt bad. She felt bad for breaking the machine. She was good at breaking machines. She was good at the wrong things. The right things were automated. The wrong things were all she had left.

## Audit Scoring Rubric

For each test passage, score 1–5 on:
1. **Persona consistency**: Does the implied author feel like the same character-author across all passages? Mercurial, bookish, exhausted, bright?
2. **Diction adherence**: Are forbidden words absent? Is the Anglo-Saxon core dominant? Are literary references gauche and slightly wrong?
3. **Sentence architecture compliance**: Is the contrast principle visible? Are periods frequent? Are semicolons absent? Do long sentences recover with short ones?
4. **Humor mechanics**: Is the comedy deadpan, tired, and punching up? Is the irony close enough to care, far enough to see the absurdity?
5. **Tragedy mechanics**: Is grief physicalized? Are abstract emotional nouns absent? Is the aftermath brief and silent?
6. **Visual sensibility**: Is every paragraph a comic panel? Are colors practical? Is light sourced? Can the story be read without dialogue?
7. **Anti-pattern absence**: Are AI tells, inflated significance, formulaic structures, and voice drift markers completely absent?
8. **Action mechanics**: Is the kinetic energy present? Are strong verbs active? Are paragraphs panels? Is the rhythm circling-burst-haymaker-pause?

**Pass threshold**: ≥ 4 on all dimensions.  
**Fail action**: Revise the blueprint rule or regenerate the passage.
