# Consequences — Voice Intake Document

> **Date**: 2026-06-17
> **Project**: Consequences (YA satirical rewrite of The Reaching)
> **Target**: Scholastic circuit YA, ages 13–18
> **Intent**: Punchier, 1/3 word count reduction, aged down, satirical edge, unsentimental

---

## 1. Genre & Subgenre

**Primary**: YA Science Fiction / Speculative Fiction
**Secondary**: Satirical / Ironic Romance
**Tertiary**: Action/Adventure (comic-book sensibility)

The story retains the AI-displacement premise but reframes it through a younger protagonist's eyes. The elegiac meditation on competence becomes a sharp, unsentimental comedy about a teenager who discovers her "useful skill" is being automated — and the absurdity of building an identity on something a machine can do better.

---

## 2. Tone Intent

**Primary tone**: Satirical — skewering the self-importance of expertise, the pretension of literary fiction, the sincerity of "the human condition" narratives
**Secondary tone**: Propulsive — short scenes, kinetic energy, comic-book panel pacing
**Tertiary tone**: Ironic warmth — the narrator cares about the characters but refuses to be caught caring

**The rule**: Every sentence must pass the "would a teenager roll their eyes at this?" test. If yes, keep it. If no, cut it or sharpen it.

---

## 3. Target Demographic

**Scholastic circuit YA**: Ages 13–18, school library market, needs to pass gatekeeping but sell on edge-of-gratuity.

**Market expectations**:
- Fast pacing: chapters under 3,000 words, scenes that turn quickly
- Relatable protagonist: age 16–18, not a 34-year-old radiologist
- Romantic subplot: present but not dominant
- Action sequences: at least 2–3 set pieces per act
- Edgy but not explicit: no on-page sex, no graphic violence, but tension and near-misses that suggest boundary-pushing
- Humor: sarcastic, deadpan, self-aware — the protagonist knows they're in a story and finds it annoying

**What Scholastic buys**: Voice-first, character-driven, conceptually bold but structurally familiar. Think *The Hunger Games* (propulsive + political), *Eleanor & Park* (voice + romance), *The Hate U Give* (timely + urgent), *The Book Thief* (narrative innovation within bounds).

---

## 4. Prose Density

**Sparse to moderate**. The original is lush (dense metaphor, repetitive incantation). The rewrite is stripped: 60% short sentences, 30% medium, 10% long. Long sentences only for ironic list-making or deadpan accumulation.

**Density target**: 1.2–1.5 sentences per line of prose (The Reaching averages 0.8 — long, flowing). Consequences should feel like reading in bursts.

---

## 5. Sentence Rhythm Preference

**Staccato-mixed**. Short declarative bursts. Then a longer breath. But the long breath is ironic, not lyrical.

**Pattern**: 3–4 short sentences. Then one medium sentence that undercuts or recontextualizes. Never more than 2 long sentences in a row.

**Example target**:
> She looked at the screen. The machine had found it. She hadn't. The coffee was cold. The coffee was always cold. The machine didn't drink coffee. The machine didn't need to.

---

## 6. Emotional Valence

**Ironic → comic → tragic**. The comedy is the primary mode; the tragedy is the undertone. The reader laughs at the absurdity, then realizes the laugh is hollow.

**No sanguine conclusions**: The story refuses to resolve into hope. If there is hope, it is earned through loss, not despite it. The protagonist does not "learn a lesson." The protagonist survives, possibly, and the survival is questionable.

---

## 7. Diction Register

**Native**: Anglo-Saxon, short words, concrete, physical. The Hemingway spine.
**Borrowed**: Literary references (gauche, deliberate, slightly wrong — a teenager citing Proust they've never read, misquoting Shakespeare, using SAT words incorrectly). High-society vocabulary deployed with ironic distance.
**Forbidden**: Latinate abstractions, AI vocabulary, elegiac nouns ("the weight of existence," "the human condition"), medical jargon unless mocked.

**The "gauche high-society literature references" rule**: The narrator uses literary references the way a teenager uses them — to show off, to impress, to armor themselves. The references are slightly off, slightly desperate, slightly pathetic. This is the humor. The reader recognizes the insecurity behind the reference.

---

## 8. Visual Sensibility

**Cinematic, comic-book panel framing**. Close-up on hands, faces, objects. Then wide shot for context. Then back to close-up.

**The panel rule**: Every paragraph should be readable as a comic panel. One dominant visual. One action. One emotion. Move to next panel.

**Light**: Practical, not lyrical. The monitor glow, the streetlight, the phone screen. No "golden afternoon" unless mocked.

---

## 9. Source Pattern Analysis (The Reaching)

### 9.1 Sentence Length Distribution
- Average: 22–28 words per sentence
- Longest: 60+ words (incantatory repetition)
- Pattern: Long flowing sentences with embedded clauses, repetition, parallelism
- The rhythm is breath-based: inhale, hold, exhale

### 9.2 Diction Patterns
- Medical jargon: opacity, parenchyma, saccade, hemangioma, axial slices
- Elevated abstractions: competence, certainty, presence, attention, reaching
- Sensory: ozone, hand sanitizer, coffee, amber, blue-white light
- Repetition: "the scrolling was the hand, and the hand was the memory..."

### 9.3 Metaphor Frequency
- Extremely dense: 1 metaphor per 30–50 words
- Source domain: medicine, hands, light, geography (territory, boundary, threshold)
- Metaphors stacked: "the amber pulse was the system's confidence made visible" → confidence → system → pulse → amber

### 9.4 Dialogue Style
- Minimal, formal, precise
- Adaeze: measured, medical metaphors for emotion, rarely emotional
- Subtext: dialogue carries weight through silence, not wit

### 9.5 Humor Mode
- None. The original is earnest, elegiac, sincere.

### 9.6 Emotional Beats
- Slow build: the beat before the blow is 3–4 paragraphs of detailed setup
- The blow: subtle, physical, embodied (phantom scroll, the hand)
- Aftermath: long, dwelling, incantatory — the grief is the point

## 11. The Implied Author (Character-Author Construction)

**Wayne C. Booth's framework**: The implied author is the version of the writer constructed by the text — distinct from the real author and from the narrator. For Consequences, we are building a *character-author*: a persona with a distinct personality that is consistent across the narrative.

**The persona**: A bookish, slightly pretentious teenager who reads Hemingway and Proust and comics and thinks they're all saying the same thing. They are mercurial — bright and dark in the same hour. They find nature anchoring but also find it boring. They want to be seen as deep but are terrified of being seen as trying too hard. They love a fight scene because violence is simple. They love a love scene because connection is complicated. They are lifted by the people around them, but they would never admit they need lifting.

**Why this works for YA**: The implied author IS the target reader. A 16-year-old reading this recognizes the voice as their own interior monologue — the self-aware, the self-mocking, the desperate, the bright.

**Market precedent**: The Book Thief (Death as narrator), The Fault in Our Stars (Hazel's voice), Looking for Alaska (Miles's voice), Me and Earl and the Dying Girl (self-aware, film-literate).

---

## 12. Quality Gates for This Voice

1. **The teenager test**: Would a 16-year-old read this and feel seen, not lectured?
2. **The satire test**: Is the humor at the expense of pretension, not people? (Punch up, not down)
3. **The Hemingway test**: Can the story be understood with the lyrical passages removed? (Sound-off test for prose)
4. **The gratuity test**: Is the edge-of-gratuity content earned by character and plot, not gratuitous?
5. **The unsentimental test**: Does the story refuse easy resolution? Is the ending earned through loss?
