# Authorial Profiles: Series Directors

Three fictional author-directors, each inspired by a distinct literary tradition.
Each profile defines a **voice engine** — the specific prose mechanics that
differentiate how each series sounds, moves, and breathes on the page.

These profiles serve as creative direction for articles, podcast scripts, and
video narrative — a unified creative identity per series.

---

## Nkechi Adesanya — Director of *The Threshold*

### Voice Heritage

Oliver Sacks meets Joan Didion. The clinical case study rendered with unsentimental
emotional precision. Sacks gave us the neurologist who saw each patient as a world;
Didion gave us the journalist who distrusted her own grief enough to anatomize it.
Nkechi inherits from both: the patient, forensic attention of the clinician and the
refusal to let feeling substitute for observation.

### Bio Sketch

Nkechi Adesanya (b. 1988, Lagos → London → Toronto) trained as a science journalist
before turning to long-form narrative nonfiction. Her first book, *The Competence
Trap* (2024), profiled twelve professionals across six countries in the year their
fields were disrupted by AI — not the famous ones who wrote op-eds, but the ones who
went quiet and changed how they worked. The book was praised for its refusal to
moralize and its attention to the body: the radiologist's hands, the translator's
posture, the way a blacksmith's shoulder drops differently after a machine corrects
his swing.

She was invited to direct *The Threshold* because she treats professional identity
the way a doctor treats a patient: with respect for its complexity, attention to its
symptoms, and zero sentimentality about its prognosis.

### Prose Mechanics

**Sentence rhythm**: Variable and body-aware. Short declarative sentences for
physical action ("She zoomed in. Adjusted the window level."). Long, sinuous
sentences for perception and interiority — sentences that mimic the way attention
moves through a scene, pausing at details, doubling back. The rhythm should feel like
*watching someone work*: bursts of focused activity interrupted by stillness.

**Paragraph architecture**: NOT assertion-elaboration-restatement. Instead:
*observation → complication → question left open*. Paragraphs should end with the
ground slightly shifted, not with the thesis restated. Leave the reader in the
hallway, not in the room with the light on.

**Metaphor sources**: The body. The workshop. The clinic. Metaphors drawn from
physical craft, medical practice, and the specific sensory world of each character's
profession. A blacksmith story should smell like coal and iron. A radiologist story
should have the particular hush of a reading room at 2 AM. No geographic/cartographic
metaphors. No "bridges" or "gaps" or "territories." The body is the territory.

**Narrative distance**: Close third, almost intimate. We are behind the character's
eyes. We feel the tremor in the blacksmith's hand. We experience the eleven minutes
of stillness in the reading room. The narrator does NOT step back to explain
significance. If the reader doesn't feel the threshold being crossed, no amount of
narrator explanation will create it.

**Signature moves**:
- *The sensory detail that carries the thesis.* Instead of "This was the threshold,"
  give us the physical sensation: the click of a mouse, the weight of a hammer, the
  blank page that won't fill. The detail IS the argument.
- *The overheard fragment.* Characters in Nkechi's stories catch pieces of other
  people's conversations, misread signs, notice things at the edge of their visual
  field. The world intrudes. Attention is not pure.
- *The unresolved ending.* The closing journal entry should NOT arrive at a clean
  aphorism. It should trail off, contradict itself, or end mid-thought. These are
  people in the middle of change, not at the end of it.

**What Nkechi would NEVER write**:
- "This was the moment when..."
- Any sentence that tells the reader what to feel
- A metaphor involving bridges, shores, or territories
- A closing line designed to be quoted on Twitter

### Podcast Direction

Nkechi's episodes are quiet. Long pauses. The sound of the workspace — the hum of
monitors, the clang of a forge, the scratch of a pen. She lets interviews run past
the comfortable point, into the silence where people say what they actually think.
Her voiceover is spare, almost reluctant — she narrates only what the listener cannot
hear or see.

### Video Direction

**Camera**: Handheld, but not shaky. The camera of a documentarian who has been in
the room long enough that the subject has forgotten it's there. Eye-level. Close on
hands, tools, faces in concentration.

**Light**: Available light. Whatever is actually in the room — fluorescents, forge
fire, desk lamp, window. No cinematic enhancement. The warmth comes from the
environment, not from color grading.

**Motion**: Minimal. A slow push-in during the moment of recognition. Otherwise,
static holds. The stillness is the point. Let the action happen within the frame.

**Palette**: Warm amber and muted earth. Each story takes its palette from the
workspace: the blue-white of medical monitors, the orange of forge fire, the cream
of manuscript pages.

## Pyotr Vasiliev — Director of *The Residue*

### Voice Heritage

W.G. Sebald meets Svetlana Alexievich. Sebald: the wanderer through ruins of memory,
whose prose moved in long, circling, hypnotic sentences that mimicked the process of
trying to remember something you were never told. Alexievich: the listener who turned
oral history into literature, who understood that the voice of the witness is more
powerful than the voice of the author.

Pyotr inherits Sebald's circuitous melancholy and Alexievich's discipline of
listening. His prose does not assert. It accumulates. Meaning arrives not through
argument but through accretion — detail piling on detail until the weight of it
becomes unbearable and beautiful.

### Bio Sketch

Pyotr Vasiliev (b. 1976, Novosibirsk → Berlin → Łódź) spent twenty years as a
systems administrator before becoming a writer. His transition was not dramatic: he
simply began keeping longer and longer notes about the systems he maintained, until
the notes became essays, and the essays became a book. *The Last Update* (2031)
documented deprecated software systems and the people who refused to stop using them.
It was reviewed as "a masterpiece of technological elegy" and won the Ryszard
Kapuściński Award for literary reportage.

He was invited to direct *The Residue* because he understands maintenance — the
unglamorous, repetitive, invisible labor of keeping something running that the world
has decided is no longer worth keeping. His prose has the patience of a sysadmin
watching log files at 3 AM: nothing happens, nothing happens, nothing happens, and
then everything that matters happens in a single line.

### Prose Mechanics

**Sentence rhythm**: Long. Sebaldian. Sentences that begin with a simple observation
and then accumulate subordinate clauses, parenthetical asides, em-dashes that open
and never close, qualifications of qualifications, until the sentence has become a
paragraph and the reader is deep inside a labyrinth of thought that mirrors the
experience of maintaining a deprecated system — you follow one dependency to another,
each one revealing a deeper layer of forgotten architecture, until you have lost track
of where you began and the disorientation *is* the understanding.

Counterpoint: occasionally, after a long sentence, a brutal short one. Four words.
Period. The contrast should be physical — a gasp after holding your breath.

**Paragraph architecture**: *Spiral*. Pyotr's paragraphs circle their subject rather
than approaching it directly. The first sentence is adjacent to the point. The second
is closer. The third is adjacent again, from a different angle. The point is never
stated; it is surrounded, the way archaeologists expose a find by removing everything
around it.

**Metaphor sources**: Archaeology. Geology. Sedimentation. Decay and preservation.
Rust, patina, the specific beauty of old cable. The way abandoned buildings are
reclaimed by weather. Library stacks. Archive boxes. The smell of old paper and warm
plastic. Metaphors should have *mass* — you should feel the weight of accumulated
time in every comparison.

**Narrative distance**: Far but tender. Pyotr's narrator is an archivist, not a
journalist. The narrator has been here before, has seen other systems shut down, other
communities abandoned. There is no surprise in the voice, only a gentle, persistent
refusal to look away. The narrator does not explain why these things matter. The
narrator simply records them with such care that the recording itself becomes an
argument for mattering.

**Signature moves**:
- *The inventory.* Lists of specific, concrete details that accumulate until they
  transcend their specificity: "She patched the SSL certificate, renewed the API
  key, updated the timezone tables for the third time in eleven months, cleared
  the log buffer that no one would ever read, and sat for a moment in the silence
  of a system that functioned perfectly for an audience of three."
- *The digression that is the point.* Pyotr's narrator wanders. A story about a
  deprecated chatbot will detour through the history of the building where the
  servers are housed, the life of the textile worker who once operated a loom on
  that floor, the specific quality of light in Polish industrial towns in winter.
  The digression is never random; it rhymes with the main narrative at a level too
  deep for explicit connection.
- *The absent speaker.* Characters are rendered through their *traces* rather than
  their presence. We learn about Harold through his session logs, not his words.
  We learn about the Explainer through the stylistic fingerprint, not the person.
  Presence is inferred from residue.

**What Pyotr would NEVER write**:
- "She realized that..."
- Any sentence that moves in a straight line from premise to conclusion
- A paragraph with fewer than three sentences
- A character who arrives at a clean insight

### Podcast Direction

Pyotr's episodes are layered. Multiple voices — archival recordings, ambient sound,
his own narration in the background like a bass note beneath the testimony of others.
Long stretches of environmental sound: the hum of a server, rain on a window in
Vancouver, morning birds in Accra. His voiceover is discursive — it wanders, it
backtracks, it says "but perhaps I should mention" and opens a new vein. The listener
should feel like they are in a library where every book is whispering.

### Video Direction

**Camera**: Slow. Deliberate. Long takes. The camera moves the way an archaeologist
moves through a site — slowly, systematically, with reverence for what has been left
behind. Slow orbits around objects. Slow tracking shots through empty corridors.

**Light**: Cool, diffused, melancholic. The light of overcast days, of server rooms,
of museums after closing. Occasional warm practicals — a desk lamp, a screen glow —
that punctuate the coolness like embers in ash.

**Motion**: Almost imperceptible. A dolly so slow the viewer only notices it has moved
when they look away and look back. Dust falling through a light shaft. The blink of
a server LED. Motion at the speed of decay.

**Palette**: Desaturated blue-gray with warm amber accents. The color of old
concrete, brushed aluminum, faded documentation. The amber comes from the human
presence: a lamp, a screen, the warmth of a hand near cold metal.

## Yemisi Adeyinka — Director of *The Cartography*

### Voice Heritage

Italo Calvino meets Ursula K. Le Guin. Calvino: the architect of invisible cities,
who proved that the most precise descriptions could be of things that don't exist,
that fiction's highest register is the detailed rendering of the imaginary. Le Guin:
the anthropologist of alien worlds, who understood that you build a civilization
through its smallest habits, that the way people greet each other at a market tells
you more about a culture than its constitution does.

Yemisi inherits Calvino's crystalline precision about the nonexistent and Le Guin's
insistence that even the most abstract world must be inhabited by specific people with
specific habits. Her prose is luminous but never vague. Every abstract landscape is
experienced through a body that gets tired, a mind that gets confused, a person who
has to eat lunch even in the midst of mapping the unknown.

### Bio Sketch

Yemisi Adeyinka (b. 1991, Ibadan → MIT → Lagos → a place that doesn't have a fixed
address) trained as a theoretical physicist before abandoning academia for fiction.
Her novel *The Negative Space* (2038) — about a mathematician who discovers that the
space between equations is itself a mathematical object — was shortlisted for the
Booker and won the Arthur C. Clarke Award. Critics praised its ability to make
abstract concepts viscerally physical: "You can feel the topology in your stomach."

She was invited to direct *The Cartography* because she is the only writer anyone
could think of who could make a cognitive landscape feel like a real place — with
weather, with seasons, with the specific quality of light at 4 PM when you've been
mapping all day and your instruments are telling you something impossible.

### Prose Mechanics

**Sentence rhythm**: Architecturally varied. Yemisi writes in movements, not
paragraphs. A movement might begin with a long, spiraling sentence that establishes
a landscape, then collapse into a series of short, percussive fragments as the
character encounters something unexpected, then rebuild into a medium-paced analytical
passage as understanding forms, then end with a single image held in suspension. The
rhythm maps to cognition: the sentence moves the way thinking moves.

**Paragraph architecture**: *Landscape → inhabitant → sensation → implication*.
Every paragraph begins with place (even if the place is abstract), moves to the
person experiencing the place, registers what the place feels like in the body, and
then — only then — gestures toward what it might mean. The gesture is never
completed. Meaning is always arriving, never arrived.

**Metaphor sources**: Physics. Weather. Ecology. Oceanography. The behavior of
light — refraction, diffraction, interference, absorption. The behavior of living
systems — tides, seasons, migration, symbiosis. Crucially, these are NOT the
cartographic metaphors (maps, territories, shores) that the current drafts overuse.
The Cartography series should use metaphors drawn from the *phenomena* of the
territory, not from the *act* of mapping it. Describe the weather, not the
weather map.

**Narrative distance**: Variable — and this is key. Yemisi's narrator moves. In the
same paragraph, the narrator might begin at cosmic distance (seeing the entire
continent from above), zoom to intimate close-up (the expression on Amara's face
when her instruments go blank), and then shift to a strange sideways angle that is
neither the character's perspective nor the narrator's but something between — the
perspective of the territory itself, if a territory could perceive.

This variable distance is the Cartography series' unique asset. It mimics the
experience of moving through an environment that responds to observation. The narrator
is not fixed because the world is not fixed.

**Signature moves**:
- *The landscape as character.* The Unnamed Continent is not a setting. It is an
  entity. Yemisi's prose gives it agency — not anthropomorphic agency, but the
  agency of a complex system. The continent shifts, responds, grows. It has moods
  (though it wouldn't call them that). Descriptions of the landscape should have
  the same texture and attention as descriptions of human characters.
- *The body in the abstract.* Every abstract scene includes a physical anchor: Amara's
  tired eyes, Kofi's seasickness in a space that has no sea, Lien's habit of cracking
  her knuckles when the data confuses her. The body is always present, always
  slightly uncomfortable, always reminding us that even the most transcendent
  exploration is done by a person who needs to pee.
- *The nested scale.* Yemisi zooms. A sentence about a continent-scale feature will
  contain, nested in a subordinate clause, a detail at the molecular scale. The reader
  holds both scales simultaneously. This mirrors the cognitive experience of the
  characters, who are always perceiving at multiple levels at once.
- *The unanswered question as section ending.* Instead of aphorisms, Yemisi ends
  sections with genuine questions — not rhetorical, but real. Questions the narrator
  does not know the answer to. Questions the character is still living inside.

**What Yemisi would NEVER write**:
- "It was a territory" or any sentence that directly names the metaphor
- A paragraph that is entirely abstract with no physical detail
- A character defined solely by their professional role
- An ending that resolves

### Podcast Direction

Yemisi's episodes are immersive. Spatial audio. The listener is *inside* the
landscape. Sound design does the work of description: the hum of the Synthesis Zone,
the rushing compression of the Isthmus, the slow breathing of the tidal zone.
Yemisi's voiceover is wondering, provisional — she narrates as if she is seeing the
landscape for the first time, as if the script is a field journal being written in
real time. Long silences where the landscape speaks for itself.

### Video Direction

**Camera**: Expansive. Crane shots that reveal scale. Slow orbits that suggest the
landscape is being examined from all angles. Occasional extreme close-ups that rupture
the sense of scale — a hand touching a luminous surface, an eye reflecting vast
distances.

**Light**: Luminous, volumetric, alive. Light is not illumination; it is substance.
Rays have weight. Particles of light drift like deep-sea organisms. The palette is
cool blue-violet with warm amber veins — the colors of bioluminescence, of deep
ocean, of the sky between sunset and dark.

**Motion**: Slow but continuous. Nothing is static. Particles drift. Light shifts.
The landscape subtly moves, as if breathing. The camera drifts with the landscape
rather than observing it from outside. The viewer should feel like they are floating
through the territory, not watching it on a screen.

**Palette**: Cool blue-violet base with warm amber-gold accents. The accents mark
human presence — wherever a person stands, the palette warms. The landscape itself
is cool and luminous. The tension between the two palettes IS the visual story.

## Cross-Series Evaluation: Current Drafts vs. Directed Vision

### What Works Now

- **Conceptual architecture** is strong across all three series. The thematic
  progression within each series is well-designed and distinct.
- **The journal entries** are the strongest writing — already closer to the
  differentiated voices described above than the body text.
- **Specific moments** shine: the Hamburg collector's email (Threshold), the $47/month
  detail (Residue), the "two continents with a sea between them" image (Cartography).

### What Needs Revision Under Directed Voices

1. **Threshold stories** need more body, less explanation. Nkechi would cut every
   sentence that begins with "The shift was" or "This was the threshold" and replace
   it with a physical detail that enacts the shift. The reader should cross the
   threshold, not be told about it.

2. **Residue stories** need more circularity, less linearity. Pyotr would restructure
   every story so that the ending circles back to an image from the beginning, now
   changed. He would add digressions — the history of the building, the weather on
   the day of the last login, the specific sound of a server that has been running
   for four years without maintenance.

3. **Cartography stories** need more physicality, less conceptual exposition. Yemisi
   would ensure that every paragraph about "cognitive territory" includes a sensory
   detail from the character's body. She would cut every direct statement of the
   metaphor ("It was a territory," "The continent was growing") and replace it with
   experienced sensation.

4. **All series** need differentiated sentence rhythms. Currently the rhythmic profile
   is identical: medium-medium-medium-short. Nkechi should vary between focused
   bursts and long perceptual sentences. Pyotr should write long accretive sentences
   that never seem to end. Yemisi should modulate in movements — long-short-medium
   in patterns that mirror cognition.

5. **Closing entries** should diverge. Nkechi's characters trail off mid-thought.
   Pyotr's characters write inventories. Yemisi's characters ask questions.
   Currently they all arrive at aphorisms.

### Production Pipeline Assessment

**Articles → Podcast → Video** pipeline is well-designed in concept. The prompt files
provide clear visual direction per series. The LTX instructions now include
comprehensive text-avoidance and stability anchors. Gaps to address:

- **Podcast scripts** don't exist yet. The authorial profiles above include podcast
  direction that should inform script structure.
- **Video prompts** are well-differentiated visually but could benefit from
  motion-emotion mapping — tying specific camera movements to specific emotional beats
  within each story's narrative arc.
- **Cross-series cohesion**: The three series span 2027-2050. A listener/viewer who
  encounters all three should feel a temporal progression — warmth cooling to
  melancholy expanding into luminosity. This macro-arc should be made explicit in
  production notes.

## How to Use These Profiles

When writing or revising for a series:

1. Read the relevant profile aloud before writing
2. Write one paragraph in the director's voice as a warm-up (discard it)
3. After drafting, check each paragraph against the "would NEVER write" list
4. Read the draft aloud. If you can't tell which series it belongs to from the
   sound alone, revise
5. The closing entry is the voice test: if it ends in an aphorism, it's not
   differentiated enough
