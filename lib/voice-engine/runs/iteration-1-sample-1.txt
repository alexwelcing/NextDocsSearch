The sommelier held the glass at a fifteen-degree angle, the way she had ten thousand times, and watched the wine crawl up the side. Legs. She was looking at the legs. The fluorescent overheads in the tasting room buzzed at a frequency she'd stopped hearing years ago.

The AI had already posted its analysis. She could see it on the tablet propped against the decanter: a full aromatic profile, acidity estimate, probable varietal blend, vintage range. Fourteen seconds from pour to printout. She hadn't even gotten the glass to her nose.

She got it there now. Inhaled. Blackcurrant, yes. Something earthy beneath it — wet slate, maybe, or the clay her father used to bring home on his boots from the vineyard in Stellenbosch. The AI's printout said "cassis, mineral, tertiary leather notes." Close. Not wrong. But the wet slate wasn't mineral and the thing she smelled beneath the blackcurrant wasn't leather — it was a place. A specific place on a specific afternoon when she was nine.

She set the glass down.

The tasting room was cold. March in London, the heating system in this building running on a schedule designed for office workers who left at six, not sommeliers who tasted at ten PM because the palate was freshest when the city quieted down. She pulled her cardigan tighter. The wool caught on a callus on her left thumb — a ridge of hard skin from years of working corkscrews, a small topography that no one else could read.

On the tablet, the AI was right. Technically, forensically, analytically right. It had identified the blend — sixty percent Cabernet Sauvignon, twenty-five Merlot, fifteen Cabernet Franc — within tolerances she couldn't match on her best day. It had estimated the vintage as 2019, which was correct. It had flagged the slightly elevated volatile acidity, which she had also caught but hadn't yet articulated.

She poured a second glass. This one she didn't analyze. She drank it, slowly, standing in the cold room with the fluorescents buzzing, tasting not the wine but the distance between what the machine measured and what her mouth remembered. The distance wasn't quality. She wasn't better than the machine. The distance was biography. The machine tasted molecules. She tasted Stellenbosch.

Her assistant came in at eleven to ask if she'd finished the evaluations. She was sitting on the floor with her back against the tasting bench, the tablet dark, two empty glasses beside her.

"Not yet," she said. She didn't say what she was thinking, which was that the evaluations were finished and what remained was something the evaluation form didn't have a field for.
