The Severance Botanist: A Counselor Prescribes Living Things

2029-10-22 · 8 min read · Alex Welcing

A corporate outplacement counselor gives each displaced professional a plant to tend — not as therapy but as a mirror. The engineer gets a bonsai. The radiologist gets a species diagnosed only by touch. Six months later, her clients are healthier than the ones who found new jobs. Then AI summarizes her paper and distributes it worldwide, stripped of every nuance that made it work.

**October 2029**

Ruth Akerman had been a corporate outplacement counselor for nine years, which meant she had spent nine years helping people who had been fired find new ways to describe themselves. She was good at this. She had a gift for hearing the competence inside the grief, the transferable skill hiding behind the industry-specific title. She could turn "I was a senior actuarial analyst for twenty years" into "I model uncertainty for a living" in a single session.

But in 2029, her clients stopped being people who had lost jobs. They became people who had lost *the thing they were*.

The difference was not semantic. A person who has lost a job needs a new job. A person who has lost what they are needs something Ruth's training had not prepared her for, and the outplacement industry's toolkit — résumé workshops, networking events, LinkedIn optimization — was as useful to these people as a map of a city that no longer exists.

Ruth knew this because her clients told her. Not in so many words. In the quality of their silence when she suggested they update their profile. In the way they held their coffee cups with both hands, as if the warmth were doing work their careers used to do.

---

**The first plant**

The idea came from failure. A client — a structural engineer named David who had designed bridges for thirty years and whose spatial reasoning had been surpassed by a generative design system — sat in her office for fifty minutes and said nothing that either of them believed. The résumé was updated. The talking points were rehearsed. The networking strategy was outlined. David nodded through all of it with the courtesy of a man who understood that Ruth was doing her job and that her job, like his, was becoming a performance of itself.

At the end of the session, Ruth looked at the bonsai on her windowsill — a juniper she had been training for six years, weekly trimming, constant attention to the tension between growth and shape — and said, without planning to: "Take this home."

David looked at her.

"It needs trimming every three days. You have to read the growth pattern and decide what to cut. The decisions are spatial. If you cut wrong, you can't undo it. The tree will show your choices for years."

David took the bonsai. He came back the following week holding it with both hands, the same way he had held his coffee, but differently — the grip of someone carrying a living thing that depended on his judgment.

"I trimmed it," he said. "I cut a branch I shouldn't have."

"How do you know?"

"Because the balance shifted. The left side is heavier now. I'll need to compensate over the next two months."

It was the first sentence he had spoken in weeks that sounded like him.

---

**The matching**

Ruth began matching plants to professionals. Not randomly — with the precision of someone who had spent nine years listening to what people needed and now, for the first time, had an instrument that could provide it.

The supply chain optimizer got a climbing vine that required daily decisions about which tendrils to redirect and which to let wander. The pathologist got a collection of succulents whose health could only be assessed by the color and firmness of their leaves — a diagnosis made by touch. The radiologist got a species of orchid whose roots were translucent: you could see the internal hydration state, but only if you held it at the right angle in the right light. Reading the roots was, Ruth explained, not unlike reading a scan.

The architect got a terrarium — a closed system where every element interacted, where changing one thing changed everything, where the design was never finished because the organisms kept growing.

The translator got a grafted fruit tree: two species joined at a wound, each contributing something the other lacked, neither complete alone.

Ruth did not call this therapy. She called it "something to tend." The outplacement firm did not object because the plants were cheap, the clients were calmer, and the firm's satisfaction metrics improved by eighteen percent.

---

**The six-month observation**

At six months, Ruth compiled her observations. She had placed plants with forty-three clients. She had a control group of sixty-one clients who had received standard outplacement services.

The data was clear and strange. Her plant clients had lower anxiety scores, higher self-reported purpose, and — the number that made Ruth stop and reread her own spreadsheet — lower rates of reemployment.

They were healthier and less employed.

She dug into the data. The standard clients who had found new jobs described them in instrumental terms: "It pays the bills," "It's something to do," "I'm keeping busy." The plant clients who had not found new jobs described their days in terms that Ruth's professional vocabulary classified as "meaningful engagement with complexity" — the exact thing their careers used to provide and the job market no longer could.

David, the structural engineer, had joined a community garden and was teaching other displaced professionals to graft trees. The pathologist had started a small succulent nursery on her balcony and was selling plants at a weekend market. The translator had begun writing a blog about grafting as metaphor — the wound, the joining, the new fruit that neither parent could produce alone.

None of them had updated their LinkedIn profiles.

---

**The paper**

Ruth wrote a paper. She wrote it carefully, over three months, in the evenings after her last client left. The paper was called "Tending as Transition: Embodied Engagement and Vocational Identity After Professional Displacement." It was twenty-two pages long. It contained the data. But the data was not the point.

The point was in the case studies — the specific, irreducible stories of specific people finding their way back to competence through the daily practice of caring for something that depended on their judgment. The point was that David's bonsai required the same spatial reasoning his bridges had required, but at a scale and speed that let him feel the reasoning in his hands rather than delegate it to software. The point was that the pathologist's succulents gave her back the diagnostic act — the reading of surfaces for evidence of invisible interior states — in a form no AI had claimed.

The point was that the plants were not substitutes for careers. They were mirrors. They reflected back to each person the specific shape of their competence, so they could see it clearly enough to carry it forward into whatever came next.

Ruth submitted the paper to the *Journal of Vocational Behavior*. It was accepted. It was published. It was read by perhaps four hundred people.

Then an AI summarization service included it in a digest for the global outplacement industry. The summary was three paragraphs. It reduced Ruth's twenty-two pages to a recommendation: "Providing displaced workers with plants may improve engagement metrics and reduce counselor burden." It cited the eighteen percent satisfaction improvement. It did not cite David, the pathologist, the translator, or the word "mirror."

Within two months, six outplacement firms had launched "Green Transition" programs. They gave displaced workers succulents. Any succulents. Without matching, without intention, without the specific, patient act of listening that let Ruth know which plant belonged to which person. The programs improved metrics by four percent, which was enough to justify the cost of the plants, which was almost nothing.

Ruth read about the programs. She sat in her office with her empty windowsill — she had given away every plant she owned — and understood that her insight had been translated, and that the translation was competent, and that the competence had stripped out the thing that mattered.

---

**October 22, 2029 — Ruth's personal journal**

*A woman called today from a firm in Singapore. She had read the summary — not the paper, the summary — and wanted to know my "protocol." She used that word. Protocol. As if tending were a procedure.*

*I tried to explain that the matching was the practice. That the plant had to reflect the person's specific form of competence. That you had to listen — really listen, for weeks — before you knew which living thing would serve as the right mirror. She asked if I had a rubric. I said the rubric was attention. She said she would call back. She will not call back.*

*The paper is in the world now, and the world has done to it what the world does: extracted the mechanism, discarded the meaning, scaled the husk. It's a small version of what happened to my clients — their expertise extracted, optimized, repackaged without the human tissue that gave it life.*

*I wonder if this is how the threshold feels from the inside. Not the big moment — not the scan that the machine reads better — but the slow corrosion. Your best insight, summarized. Your nuance, averaged. Your life's work, reduced to a bullet point in someone else's deck.*

*The plants are still growing. David's bonsai has recovered from the bad cut. The pathologist's succulents are propagating — she gives the cuttings away with handwritten care instructions. The translator's grafted tree bore fruit last month. She brought me one. It tasted like nothing I had eaten before.*

*I ate it alone in my office, on the empty windowsill, and it was the best thing I'd tasted in years.*
