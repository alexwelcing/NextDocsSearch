# The Subsidy

**September 2033**

The letter from the Ministry of Cultural Preservation arrived in August, printed on the heavy paper that government communications used when they wanted to convey permanence, and it informed Signe Lindqvist that her application for Heritage Practitioner Status had been approved.

She was, as of September 1, 2033, an officially recognized practitioner of traditional furniture making, eligible for a monthly stipend of 14,200 Swedish kronor — approximately €1,200 — for as long as she continued to practice her craft and submitted quarterly documentation of her work to the Ministry's Heritage Skills Registry.

Signe read the letter in her workshop in Mora, a small town in Dalarna where her family had made furniture for four generations. The workshop smelled of pine shavings and hide glue and the particular iron tang of hand-forged tools that had been oiled and stored and oiled again for decades. Her grandmother's spokeshave hung on the wall above the lathe. Her father's marking gauge sat on the bench, its brass face worn to a mirror by fifty years of thumbs.

She had applied for the subsidy because she needed the money. AI-designed, robotically manufactured furniture had captured eighty percent of the Swedish market. Her remaining customers were either loyalists — people who had bought from the Lindqvist workshop for years and continued out of habit or affection — or a newer category she had trouble naming: people who wanted handmade furniture not because it was better but because it was handmade. The distinction bothered her, though she could not yet articulate why.

She signed the acceptance form. She submitted her first quarterly documentation: photographs of four chairs, a dining table, and a chest of drawers, accompanied by written descriptions of the materials, joinery techniques, and approximate hours of labor.

The Ministry responded with a certificate suitable for display.

---

**The display**

The certificate was the first sign. It was designed to be hung in the workshop — visible to visitors, Signe assumed, as a mark of authenticity. It read: *This workshop is recognized by the Ministry of Cultural Preservation as a Heritage Skill Practice Site.*

Visitors began arriving. Not customers — visitors. They came on weekends, often in small groups, sometimes with children. They watched Signe work. They asked questions about the joinery, the wood selection, the hand tools. They photographed the workshop. Several asked if they could photograph her hands.

Signe allowed it, the first few times, with the mild discomfort of someone who understood that she was being perceived as a specimen. She was doing the same work she had always done — mortise-and-tenon joints, hand-planed surfaces, steam-bent backs — but the context had shifted. Her workshop was no longer a place where furniture was made. It was a place where the making of furniture was observed.

A woman from Stockholm asked if Signe offered "experiences." Signe said no. The woman clarified: she wanted to spend a day in the workshop, using the tools, making something small. A cutting board, perhaps. She would pay for the experience.

Signe said no again, more firmly, and the firmness surprised her, because the offer was reasonable and the money would have been useful. What was not reasonable was the word "experience." The word transformed her daily practice — the repetitive, exhausting, unglamorous labor of making functional objects from wood — into something consumable. An experience is something you have and leave. Signe's work was something she lived inside. The difference was not negotiable.

---

**The chairs**

The subsidy changed the chairs. Not immediately. Not in any way that Signe could see in the wood or the joinery or the finish. The chairs were the same chairs she had always made — simple, sturdy, designed to be sat in daily for decades.

But the chairs were no longer received as chairs.

A couple from Gothenburg bought two dining chairs in November. They sent Signe a photograph of the chairs in their apartment. The chairs were displayed on a low platform, against a white wall, lit by a gallery light. The couple had not placed them at their dining table. They had placed them as objects — as sculptures, as statements, as things to be looked at rather than sat upon.

Signe stared at the photograph. The chairs were beautiful. She did not dispute that. They were also chairs. They were designed to hold a human body while that body ate dinner, read a newspaper, argued with a spouse, leaned back in satisfaction or forward in grief. They were designed to creak in specific ways that told you, over years, about the shifting of your own weight, your habits, your posture as you aged.

On a platform, against a white wall, the chairs said nothing about any of this. They said: *Look at what a human made.*

Signe wrote to the couple and asked, gently, if they might consider putting the chairs at their table. The couple responded that they had an AI-designed dining set that was more ergonomically optimized. The Lindqvist chairs were "too precious to use daily."

*Too precious to use.*

Signe put the letter down. She went into the workshop and sat in one of her own chairs — a prototype from last year, slightly irregular, with a joint in the left rear leg that she knew was a quarter-millimeter loose — and she sat in it because it was a chair and sitting was what it was for and the sitting was the entire meaning.

---

**The quarterly report**

The Ministry's quarterly documentation form asked for photographs of completed work, descriptions of techniques employed, and a brief narrative about "the cultural significance of the heritage practice."

Signe wrote her first three quarterly narratives dutifully. She described the joinery. She explained the wood-selection process. She noted the regional traditions her work drew upon.

For the fourth quarterly report, due in September 2033, she wrote something different.

*I make chairs. I have made chairs for twenty-two years, as my father did for forty, as his mother did for thirty-seven before him. The chairs are for sitting. They are not for display, not for photography, not for the cultural enrichment of visitors from Stockholm who want to watch me use a spokeshave as though the spokeshave were a relic and I were a reenactor.*

*The subsidy has made me a heritage practitioner. I do not want to be a heritage practitioner. I want to be a furniture maker. The difference is: a heritage practitioner preserves a practice. A furniture maker practices it. Preservation implies that the thing is dying and must be kept alive artificially, the way a museum preserves a species in formaldehyde. Practice implies that the thing is alive and useful and ongoing.*

*My chairs are alive and useful and ongoing. People can sit in them for fifty years. The joints will hold. The wood will darken. The seats will develop the shallow depressions that map a particular body's particular habits. This is what furniture is for. Not memory. Not heritage. Use.*

*I am returning the subsidy. I will continue to make chairs. I will sell them to people who intend to sit in them. If this means I cannot afford the workshop, I will make smaller chairs. But I will not make museum pieces. I will not make heritage. I will make chairs.*

---

**After**

Signe cancelled the Heritage Practitioner Status in October. She removed the Ministry certificate from her wall. She left a faint rectangular outline where the certificate had hung, the wood slightly lighter from years of UV protection, and she did not fill the outline because it served as a reminder of the year she had allowed someone else to define what her work meant.

She sold eleven chairs that winter, at prices that did not cover her material costs. Nine of the eleven buyers placed them at tables. One used hers as a bedside reading chair. One put his on a porch, where it weathered and grayed and became, over the following years, the most beautiful chair Signe had ever made — not because of anything she had done, but because of what the rain and the sun and the sitting had done.

The subsidy program continued. Seventeen hundred heritage practitioners across Sweden received monthly stipends. Glassblowers, weavers, blacksmiths, cobblers, letterpress printers. Some were grateful. Some felt what Signe felt. None of them had a word for it until Katrin Möller's term arrived from Berlin, carried on the wind of a *Die Zeit* article: *vocational bereavement* — the grief of intact competence without functional context.

Signe read the term and thought it was close but not right. She was not bereaved. She was misclassified. The world had looked at her work and seen heritage. She looked at her work and saw furniture. The disagreement was not emotional. It was ontological. She and the world no longer agreed on what a chair was for.
